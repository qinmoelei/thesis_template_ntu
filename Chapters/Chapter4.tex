\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:earnhft_intro}

High-frequency trading (HFT) uses computer algorithms to make trading decisions at very short time scales and accounts for a substantial share of activity in modern financial markets~\cite{hhu032}. It is especially prevalent in cryptocurrency markets~\cite{ALMEIDA2023100785}, whose round-the-clock trading removes market-closure gaps and non-tradable periods---although positions remain continuously exposed to price risk---and whose volatility creates short-lived trading opportunities. These features motivate strategies that react more frequently than conventional low-frequency systems.

Reinforcement learning has produced promising results on lower-frequency tasks such as daily or intraday stock and futures trading~\cite{sun2022deepscalper,briola2021deep}, but moving to second-level decisions introduces two difficulties identified in \sref{sec:bg_gaps} as Gap~I.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{The horizon is extremely long, so RL is data-inefficient.} Even twelve days at second resolution gives roughly one million steps---$60\times 60\times 24\times 12 = 1{,}036{,}800$---against the few-thousand-step horizon of Atari benchmarks~\cite{mnih2013playing}. Such horizons demand far more data to converge~\cite{zhang2023towards}, straining computation.
\item \textbf{The market drifts, so one policy need not transfer uniformly.} Training and testing periods can have different return dynamics. A policy fitted under one trend can therefore lose performance when the trend reverses.
\end{itemize}

This chapter presents EarnHFT, an \textbf{E}fficient hier\textbf{A}rchical \textbf{R}einforcement lear\textbf{N}ing method for \textbf{H}igh \textbf{F}requency \textbf{T}rading, whose three stages are summarised in \fref{fig:earnhft_pipeline}.\footnote{This chapter has been published in the \emph{Proceedings of the AAAI Conference on Artificial Intelligence (AAAI'24)}~\cite{qin2024earnhft}.} The design follows the decompose--specialise--aggregate pattern of \sref{sec:intro_pattern}.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Stage~I (Q-teacher).} Under the historical-replay assumption that the agent's simulated orders do not alter the recorded future prices, we compute by dynamic programming the best continuation values on each realised training path. Used as a regulariser and a source of hindsight-greedy demonstrations, this ``Q-teacher'' supplies additional low-level learning signals.
\item \textbf{Stage~II (specialist pool).} Four preference-conditioned training runs produce 200 candidate checkpoints. We label the validation market with a fixed segmentation configuration and select a small pool of specialists by their observed validation performance.
\item \textbf{Stage~III (router).} We train a minute-level router that dynamically selects a second-level specialist from the pool as the observed features change.
\end{enumerate}

Across the four reported cryptocurrency test paths, spanning bull, bear, and sideways periods, EarnHFT has the largest tabled total return among the six baselines on every path and the largest risk-adjusted metrics on three. These are single-seed, nine-day point estimates rather than population-level performance claims.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:earnhft_formulation}

The financial concepts---limit order book, OHLC, technical indicators, market order, position, and net value---are defined in \sref{sec:bg_instruments}. We restrict attention to a single asset with long-only positions, and the objective is to maximise net value $V_t=V_{ct}+H_t\, p_t^{b_1}$, where $V_{ct}$ is cash and $H_t$ the position valued at the best bid. In the historical simulator, a target-position change of $\Delta$ is priced immediately against the recorded book, level by level, and $E_t(\Delta)$ denotes the resulting commission-inclusive simulated cash flow---the signed cash amount, not an average execution price. A buy ($\Delta>0$) consumes the recorded ask side,
\[
E_t(\Delta)=\sum_{i=1}^{4} \bigl(p_t^{a_i}\min(q_t^{a_i},R_{i-1})\bigr)(1+\sigma),
\]
whereas a sell ($\Delta<0$) consumes the recorded bid side with the commission deducted from the proceeds,
\[
E_t(\Delta)=-\sum_{i=1}^{4} \bigl(p_t^{b_i}\min(q_t^{b_i},R_{i-1})\bigr)(1-\sigma),
\]
where $R_0=|\Delta|$ and, on the relevant side of the book, $R_i=R_{i-1}-\min(q_t^{a_i},R_{i-1})$ for a buy and $R_i=R_{i-1}-\min(q_t^{b_i},R_{i-1})$ for a sell. Thus $R_{i-1}$ is the quantity still unfilled after the first $i-1$ levels. Here $\sigma$ is the commission rate and $E_t(0)=0$. The released action-availability calculation sums the first four levels; a change requiring more displayed depth is masked rather than extrapolated beyond them. Under this convention a buy is a positive cash outflow and a sell a negative one. This is an accounting model over recorded snapshots, not a live execution engine: it assumes immediate access to displayed depth and no latency or market impact. Accordingly, ``high-frequency trading'' in this chapter means second-level target-position control in historical replay; order placement and cancellation, queue priority, realistic partial-fill dynamics, latency, and endogenous impact are outside its scope.

\subsection{A Hierarchical Markov Decision Process}
\label{sec:earnhft_hmdp}

The two challenges of \sref{sec:earnhft_intro} motivate a temporal decomposition: micro-level features support second-by-second target-position decisions, while their minute-level aggregation supplies a slower signal for switching among specialists. We therefore formulate a hierarchical MDP $(\mathrm{MDP}_h,\mathrm{MDP}_l)$, in which a low-level MDP applies the replay cash-flow model at second scale and a high-level MDP selects a specialist at minute scale:
\begin{align*}
\mathrm{MDP}_h &= (S_h,A_h,P_h,R_h,\gamma_h,T_h), &
\mathrm{MDP}_l &= (S_l,A_l,P_l,R_l,\gamma_l,T_l).
\end{align*}

\paragraph{Low level (second scale).} The low-level network receives the current row of 54 selected micro-level features, together with the current position $H_t$. The released preprocessing constructs trailing OHLC and LOB features with lookbacks of 5, 10, 30, and 60 seconds; the implementation's state-stack length is one, so ``length 60'' denotes the maximum feature lookback rather than a 60-row tensor supplied to the network. LOB-derived features use the first five displayed levels. The action $a_{lt}$ is a target position chosen from a finite pool $\{0,\frac{H}{|A|-1},\dots,H\}$; target positions outside the change supported by the first four displayed depth levels are masked, and the teacher assigns an effectively prohibitive value to an unfillable change. Otherwise the simulator prices the change through the replay cash-flow model above. The reward $r_{lt}=H_{t+1}p_{t+1}^{b_1}-(H_t p_t^{b_1}+E_t(H_{t+1}-H_t))$ is the simulated per-second change in net value.

\paragraph{High level (minute scale).} The high-level network analogously receives the current row of 19 selected macro-level features constructed from trailing minute OHLC, together with $H_t$. The action $a_{ht}$ selects an agent from the pre-trained pool $A_h$. The reward $r_{hT}=\sum_{t=T}^{T+\tau_m-1}r_{lt}$, where $\tau_m=60$ seconds is the length of one minute, is the simulated net-value change accrued by the selected low-level agent over those $\tau_m$ steps. Every minute the router picks a frozen low-level specialist, which may adjust the target position every second; the specialists are trained first and the router afterwards rather than by joint optimisation.

\paragraph{Options view and information timing.} The high level can be viewed as a semi-Markov decision process~\cite{sutton1999between,pateria2021hierarchical}: each action hands control to a selected specialist for $\tau_m$ low-level steps. The reported code sets both discounts to one, $\gamma_l=\gamma_h=1$, so the within-option reward is the undiscounted sum $r_{hT}$ above; the scripts used for the reported runs do not override these defaults. The data alignment is causal with respect to OHLC completion: after aggregation, one-second bars are shifted forward by one second and one-minute bars by one minute before they are merged with the order-book stream, so a row time-stamped $t$ contains bars that have ended by $t$, not a bar still forming after the decision. Minute bars are forward-filled between minute boundaries, and all rolling features are trailing. The contemporaneous LOB snapshot and these completed-bar features are read before the simulator advances to the next second.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:earnhft_method}

\subsection{Stage I: Efficient Reinforcement Learning with a Q-Teacher}
\label{sec:earnhft_stage1}

A long trajectory normally inflates the cost of RL. Under the simulator's exogenous-replay assumption, however, the recorded future prices do not change with the agent's action. Dynamic programming can therefore construct a \emph{hindsight dynamic-programming teacher}: a table $Q^\star$ containing the best simulated continuation value on each realised training path, indexed by timestamp, previous position, and next target position~\cite{sutton2018reinforcement}. Three boundaries are essential. First, $Q^\star$ is a sample-path hindsight benchmark, not the optimal action value of the unknown stochastic market; the star is retained for continuity with the published notation and always has this trajectory-conditioned meaning here. Second, its construction consumes future training prices, but the table is used only to generate training targets and demonstrations and is unavailable during validation, testing, and deployment. Third, the network state $(\chi,p)$ does not contain the timestamp, so identical feature states can receive different hindsight targets on different visits. The table is therefore used as an auxiliary regulariser and demonstrator, following the general precedent of learning from demonstrations~\cite{hester2018dqfd}, rather than as a value function that the deployed network is expected to reproduce exactly.

\paragraph{Hindsight-value supervisor.} Because the position set is finite, the teacher values can be computed backwards. In the reported configuration, $\gamma_l=1$, so each entry is the immediate simulated reward plus the best undiscounted continuation on that same sample path, as in Algorithm~\ref{alg:earnhft_qtable}.

The temporal-difference error updates the value of the sampled action, whereas the table and a network forward pass each provide a vector over all target positions. The implementation converts these vectors into action distributions and minimises the divergence from the teacher distribution to the network distribution:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:earnhft_loss}
\begin{aligned}
\mu^\star_{t,p}&:=\mathrm{softmax}\bigl(Q^\star[t,p,\cdot]/\tau_{\mathrm{sm}}\bigr),\\
\mu_{\theta_i}(\chi,p)&:=\mathrm{softmax}\bigl(Q_{\theta_i}(\chi,p,\cdot)/\tau_{\mathrm{sm}}\bigr),\\
L(\theta_i)&=L_{td}+\alpha_{\mathrm{KL}}\,
\mathrm{KL}\bigl(\mu^\star_{t,p}\,\|\,\mu_{\theta_i}(\chi,p)\bigr),
\qquad \tau_{\mathrm{sm}}=1.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
For a sampled transition collected at historical time $t$, the first distribution is therefore a direct lookup in the trajectory-indexed table; it is not a function of $\chi$. Writing $d$ for the terminal indicator, the temporal-difference term is
\[
\begin{aligned}
y_i^{\mathrm{TD}}&=r+\gamma_l(1-d)\max_b Q_{\theta_i'}(\chi',a,b),\\
L_{td}&=\bigl(y_i^{\mathrm{TD}}-Q_{\theta_i}(\chi,p,a)\bigr)^2,
\qquad \gamma_l=1.
\end{aligned}
\]
The released implementation takes the maximum directly over the target network; despite the original paper's DDQN terminology, this is a target-network DQN update rather than strict Double DQN, which would select with the online network and evaluate with the target network. Here $\chi$ is the feature vector, $p$ the previous position, and $\theta_i'$ the target parameters. For the two distributions in \eqref{eq:earnhft_loss}, the code evaluates $\mathrm{KLDiv}(\log(\mu_{\theta_i}+10^{-8}),\mu^\star_{t,p}+10^{-8})$; its direction is therefore $\mathrm{KL}(\mu^\star_{t,p}\|\mu_{\theta_i})$, with $10^{-8}$ used only for numerical stability. There is no learned or tuned softmax temperature. The supervision coefficient starts at $256$ and decreases linearly to a floor of $128$ over $5\times10^6$ environment steps; it does not decay to zero. The softmax distributions exist only inside the loss, while action selection remains greedy in the network values at evaluation time.

Two properties explain what this auxiliary term matches. First, the softmax is unchanged by adding the same constant to every action value: for any $c\in\mathbb{R}$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:earnhft_softmax_shift}
\mathrm{softmax}\bigl(Q(x,\cdot)\bigr)=\mathrm{softmax}\bigl(Q(x,\cdot)-c\bigr)
\qquad\text{for every }c\in\mathbb{R}.
\end{equation}
Choosing $c=\tfrac{1}{|A|}\sum_{a\in A}Q(x,a)$ shows that each distribution depends on its action-value vector only through that vector \emph{centred across the actions}---the advantage of each action over the state's average action value---and not on a common offset. The analytical surrogate below uses an analogous centred residual $Re$; it is not the trajectory table $Q^\star$ and should not be identified with it. The KL term therefore matches relative action preferences, including their separation, rather than the common value level.

Second, removing the common offset makes the auxiliary target less restrictive than direct $L_2$ regression onto hindsight returns. It still asks the network to imitate relative preferences derived from a realised future path, so their out-of-sample usefulness is an empirical assumption, not an optimality guarantee. This is why the term is treated as regularisation rather than as supervised recovery of the market's true value function.

At every visited state this construction supplies an auxiliary gradient over all target positions, including positions not selected in that transition; the ablation in \sref{sec:earnhft_ablation} evaluates its empirical effect.

\begin{algorithm}[!htb]
\caption{Construction of the Hindsight Teacher Values}
\label{alg:earnhft_qtable}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} time series $\mathcal{D}=\{x_t\}_{t=0}^{N-1}$, commission rate $\sigma$, action space $A$, maximum holding $H$
\State \textbf{Output:} table $Q^\star$ of teacher action values indexed by time, position index, action index
\State Let $I=\{0,\ldots,|A|-1\}$ and $\mathrm{pos}(i)=\frac{iH}{|A|-1}$; initialise $Q^\star\in(\mathbb{R}\cup\{-\infty\})^{N\times|A|\times|A|}$ to zero.
\For{$t \gets N-2$ \textbf{down to} $0$}
  \For{$p \gets 0$ \textbf{to} $|A|-1$}
    \For{$a \gets 0$ \textbf{to} $|A|-1$}
      \If{$\mathrm{pos}(a)-\mathrm{pos}(p)$ cannot be filled from levels $1{:}4$}
        \State $Q^\star[t,p,a]\gets-\infty$ \Comment{masked action}
      \Else
        \State $Q^\star[t,p,a] \gets \max_{a'\in I} Q^\star[t+1,a,a'] + \mathrm{pos}(a)\,p_{t+1}^{b_1} - \bigl(\mathrm{pos}(p)\,p_{t}^{b_1} + E_t(\mathrm{pos}(a)-\mathrm{pos}(p))\bigr)$
      \EndIf
    \EndFor
  \EndFor
\EndFor
\State \textbf{return} $Q^\star$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

In Algorithm~\ref{alg:earnhft_qtable}, $p$ and $a$ are indices into the position pool, and the immediate term is the simulator's one-step marked-to-bid reward for moving from $\mathrm{pos}(p)$ to $\mathrm{pos}(a)$. The $-\infty$ notation is the mathematical form of an effectively prohibitive action mask; the no-change action remains feasible, so the maximum and the softmax normaliser always contain a finite entry. The released helper exposes a discount argument, but the training environment passes the reported default $\gamma_l=1$; the algorithm therefore shows the recursion that was actually evaluated. Its terminal row is zero and the backward loop ends at $t=0$, avoiding an out-of-range access at $t=N-1$. The resulting table is specific to the supplied historical chunk and its replay cost model.

\paragraph{Hindsight-greedy demonstrator.} In addition to $\epsilon$-greedy rollouts, the implementation collects a rollout that chooses $\argmax_a Q^\star[t,p,a]$ on the realised training chunk. These are sample-path demonstrations rather than actions available from an oracle at deployment. Algorithm~\ref{alg:earnhft_efficient} summarises the two rollout streams and their common TD-plus-KL update.

\begin{algorithm}[!htb]
\caption{Efficient Reinforcement Learning with a Q-Teacher}
\label{alg:earnhft_efficient}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} time series $\mathcal{D}$ of length $N$, commission rate $\sigma$, action space $A$, number of epochs $n_{\mathrm{ep}}$, minibatch size $B$
\State \textbf{Output:} network parameters $\theta$
\State Initialise replay buffers $R_h,R_\epsilon$, network $Q_\theta$, and target network $Q_{\theta'}$; build $Q^\star$ via Algorithm~\ref{alg:earnhft_qtable}.
\For{epoch $= 1$ \textbf{to} $n_{\mathrm{ep}}$}
  \State Reset the trading environment.
  \For{$t = 0$ \textbf{to} $N-2$} \Comment{hindsight-greedy rollout}
    \State Choose $a_h=\argmax_a Q^\star[t,p,a]$; execute it to obtain $(r,s')$ and store $(s,a_h,r,s',Q^\star[t,p,\cdot])$ in $R_h$.
  \EndFor
  \State Reset the trading environment.
  \For{$t = 0$ \textbf{to} $N-2$} \Comment{exploration rollout}
    \State Choose $a_\epsilon$ by the $\epsilon$-greedy policy; execute it to obtain $(r,s')$ and store $(s,a_\epsilon,r,s',Q^\star[t,p,\cdot])$ in $R_\epsilon$.
  \EndFor
  \For{each scheduled gradient step, once the active buffer holds at least $B$ transitions}
    \State Sample $B$ transitions from the corresponding buffer and compute $L$ by \eqref{eq:earnhft_loss}, using $r+\gamma_l(1-d)\max_a Q_{\theta'}(s',a)$ with $\gamma_l=1$.
    \State Descend on $\theta$; update the target network by $\theta'\gets\tau_{\mathrm{poly}}\theta+(1-\tau_{\mathrm{poly}})\theta'$.
  \EndFor
  \State Decay $\epsilon$ and $\alpha_{\mathrm{KL}}$, retaining the floor $\alpha_{\mathrm{KL}}\ge128$.
\EndFor
\State \textbf{return} $Q_\theta$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The following analysis is an analytical motivation, not a convergence guarantee for the released learner. It considers a tabular historical-replay MDP with state $(t,p)$ rather than the network's feature state, and replaces the implemented teacher-to-student softmax-KL by an exact centred-value matching surrogate. Shift invariance explains why both operate on relative action values, but it does not make the two objectives identical. Nor is a formal correspondence established between the implementation's scheduled $\alpha_{\mathrm{KL}}\in[128,256]$ and the fixed mixing weight $\lambda$ below. To avoid a visually ambiguous case-only distinction, the implemented trajectory table remains $Q^\star$, whereas the theoretical optimum is denoted $q^{\mathrm{opt}}$ throughout this surrogate analysis. For the discounted tabular surrogate, the operator is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:earnhft_operator}
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{T}q(x,a)={}&\lambda\sum_{y\in X}P(y\mid x,a)
 \bigl(r(x,a,y)+\gamma\max_b q(y,b)\bigr)\\
&+(1-\lambda)\Bigl(\tfrac{1}{|A|}\textstyle\sum_{a'\in A}q(x,a')+Re(x,a)\Bigr).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Here $X$ is the finite state space, $P$ the transition kernel, and $q$ the argument of the operator. The supervision residual is $Re(x,a) = q^{\mathrm{opt}}(x,a) - \tfrac{1}{|A|}\sum_{a'\in A}q^{\mathrm{opt}}(x,a')$, and $\lambda\in(0,1]$ weights the Bellman backup against the supervision term.

\begin{proposition}[The surrogate $q^{\mathrm{opt}}$ is a fixed point of $\mathcal{T}$]
\label{prop:earnhft_fixed}
Let $q^{\mathrm{opt}}$ denote the Bellman-optimal action value of the discounted tabular surrogate. Then $\mathcal{T}q^{\mathrm{opt}} = q^{\mathrm{opt}}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Substituting $q^{\mathrm{opt}}$ into \eqref{eq:earnhft_operator}, the Bellman optimality equation identifies the first term with $q^{\mathrm{opt}}(x,a)$, while the definition of $Re$ identifies the centred-value term:
\begin{align*}
\mathcal{T}q^{\mathrm{opt}}(x,a) &= \lambda\, q^{\mathrm{opt}}(x,a)
 +(1-\lambda)\Bigl(\tfrac{1}{|A|}\textstyle\sum_{a'}q^{\mathrm{opt}}(x,a')+Re(x,a)\Bigr) \\
&= \lambda\, q^{\mathrm{opt}}(x,a) + (1-\lambda)\, q^{\mathrm{opt}}(x,a)
 = q^{\mathrm{opt}}(x,a). \qedhere
\end{align*}
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Contraction in the sup-norm]
\label{prop:earnhft_contraction}
Let $\gamma\in(0,1)$. For any action values $q_1,q_2$, $\|\mathcal{T}q_1-\mathcal{T}q_2\|_\infty \le k\,\|q_1-q_2\|_\infty$ with $k=\lambda\gamma+(1-\lambda)=1-\lambda(1-\gamma)<1$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
The supervision residual $Re(x,a)$ depends only on $q^{\mathrm{opt}}$ and not on the argument of $\mathcal{T}$, so it cancels in the difference $\mathcal{T}q_1-\mathcal{T}q_2$. Bounding the $\max$ operator by $|\max_b q_1(y,b)-\max_b q_2(y,b)|\le\max_b|q_1(y,b)-q_2(y,b)|$,
\begin{align*}
\|\mathcal{T}q_1-\mathcal{T}q_2\|_\infty
&\le \lambda\gamma\,\|q_1-q_2\|_\infty + (1-\lambda)\,\|q_1-q_2\|_\infty \\
&= \bigl(\lambda\gamma+(1-\lambda)\bigr)\|q_1-q_2\|_\infty = \bigl(1-\lambda(1-\gamma)\bigr)\|q_1-q_2\|_\infty.
\end{align*}
Since $\gamma\in(0,1)$ and $\lambda\in(0,1]$, the factor $k=1-\lambda(1-\gamma)<1$. When $\gamma=1$, as in the reported experiments, the factor equals $1$ and this contraction proof no longer applies. Finite-horizon backward induction still defines the sample-path teacher in Algorithm~\ref{alg:earnhft_qtable}, but it does not establish convergence of the function-approximation learner.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Convergence of the supervised update in the tabular idealisation]
\label{thm:earnhft_convergence}
Consider the finite historical-replay MDP with state $(t,p)$, bounded rewards, and discount $\gamma\in(0,1)$. Suppose the tabular iterates driven by the operator $\mathcal{T}$ of \eqref{eq:earnhft_operator} use step sizes satisfying the Robbins--Monro conditions ($\sum_k a_k=\infty$, $\sum_k a_k^2<\infty$), every state--action pair is visited infinitely often, and the update noise has bounded variance. Then the iterates converge almost surely to the surrogate fixed point $q^{\mathrm{opt}}$.\footnote{The released learner uses function approximation, teacher-to-student softmax-KL, and $\gamma_l=1$. All three place the reported experiments outside this theorem's assumptions; the result is analytical motivation only.}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Propositions~\ref{prop:earnhft_fixed} and~\ref{prop:earnhft_contraction} show that $\mathcal{T}$ is a sup-norm contraction with unique fixed point $q^{\mathrm{opt}}$. Under the stated step-size, visitation, and noise conditions, the stochastic-approximation argument of~\cite{jaakkola1993convergence} applies, and the iterates converge almost surely to $q^{\mathrm{opt}}$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Stage II: Construction of the Agent Pool}
\label{sec:earnhft_stage2}

Because the relation between micro-level features and profitable actions changes across the sample, one network need not perform uniformly over every period. EarnHFT therefore constructs preference-conditioned candidate checkpoints and lets the validation procedure retain specialists for different labelled trends.

\paragraph{Generating diverse agents.} Rather than relying on different random seeds to create diversity~\cite{sun2023mastering}, the four training runs use explicit preferences over chunk returns. The training series (about 2.4 million steps) is divided into fixed $L=14{,}400$-second chunks; these are sampling units, not guaranteed single-trend intervals. A preference parameter $\beta$ assigns each chunk with buy-and-hold return rate $r$ a sampling priority
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:earnhft_pes}
f(r) =
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{e^{\beta r}}{\mathrm{pdf}(r)} & \text{if } Q_{\frac{\zeta}{2}}(R) \le r \le Q_{1-\frac{\zeta}{2}}(R),\\[2mm]
e^{\beta r} & \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $Q_{\frac{\zeta}{2}}(R)$ is the $\frac{\zeta}{2}$-quantile of return rates, $\zeta$ the clipping quantile, and $\mathrm{pdf}$ is a kernel density estimate,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:earnhft_kernel}
\mathrm{pdf}(x)=\frac{1}{nh}\sum_{r\in R} K\!\left(\frac{x-r}{h}\right),
\end{equation}
where $n=|R|$, bandwidth $h$ is searched around Silverman's rule~\cite{silverman1986density}, and $K$ is a standard normal kernel. Dividing by the estimated density counterweights common returns, while $e^{\beta r}$ tilts sampling towards a preferred return regime; clipping limits the influence of extreme chunks. Different $\beta$ values thereby induce different training distributions.

\paragraph{Market segmentation and labelling.} Specialist selection uses an offline label of the validation path. Algorithm~\ref{alg:earnhft_segment} applies the released code's fixed configuration to the best-bid series: a zero-phase Butterworth filter, turning-point segmentation, and normalised fastDTW merging~\cite{sakoe1978dtw,berndt1994dtw}. The same constants are used for all four markets; they are fixed implementation choices rather than parameters inferred from each dataset.

\begin{algorithm}[!htb]
\caption{Market Segmentation and Labelling}
\label{alg:earnhft_segment}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} validation best-bid series $\mathcal{D}=\{p_t^{b_1}\}_{t=0}^{N-1}$; $M=5$ labels; clipping fraction $\zeta=0.1$
\State \textbf{Output:} a trend label for every point of $\mathcal{D}$
\State $\mathcal{D}'\gets\operatorname{filtfilt}(\mathcal{D})$ using a fourth-order Butterworth low-pass filter with normalised cutoff $W_n=1/30$.
\State Split $\mathcal{D}'$ at sign changes of its first difference; absorb each segment shorter than 60 seconds into its predecessor.
\For{$k\gets1$ \textbf{to} $20$}
  \State Fit a linear slope to each remaining segment and temporarily assign five quantile labels.
  \State For each segment shorter than $3{,}600$ seconds, compute its length-and-level-normalised fastDTW distance to each adjacent candidate.
  \State Disallow a merge if the two temporary labels differ by more than one; otherwise merge with the closer neighbour when the distance is below $3\times10^{-4}$.
  \State Stop if no segment was merged in this round.
\EndFor
\State Refit each segment's normalised slope $r_i=100\,\widehat{b}_i/\mathcal{D}'_{t_i}$; collect the slopes into $R$.
\State Set $c_{\mathrm{lo}}$ and $c_{\mathrm{hi}}$ to the implementation's order-statistic approximations to $Q_{\zeta/2}(R)$ and $Q_{1-\zeta/2}(R)$.
\State Use the four equally spaced cut points from $c_{\mathrm{lo}}$ to $c_{\mathrm{hi}}$ to assign labels $0,\ldots,4$.
\State Expand each segment label to its timestamps and \textbf{return} the pointwise labels.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The distance routine is fastDTW, not the ``TWAP'' written in one version of the published appendix. It rolls the shorter segment over the longer one, averages the fastDTW distances, and divides by the shorter segment's length times its mean price. Likewise, the implementation does not test a separate continuous slope-difference tolerance: the condition is a discrete temporary-label gap of at most one plus the fastDTW threshold. Because the fourth-order filter uses \texttt{filtfilt}, it is non-causal; it is used only for offline validation segmentation and is not an online state feature.

\paragraph{Agent selection.} Including all trained agents would inflate the router's action space, so we select a small pool. Each point of the validation set is labelled by Algorithm~\ref{alg:earnhft_segment}; agents are evaluated across trends and initial positions, and the most profitable agent under each (trend, initial-position) pair is retained, forming a two-dimensional pool indexed by $(m,n)$ for $m$ trends and $n$ initial positions. Two caveats apply to this selection. The 200 candidates are checkpoints drawn from only four training runs (four preference values $\times$ 50 epochs), so many of them are highly correlated; and the same validation window is used both to select the specialists and to select the router, so the selection is exposed to a winner's-curse effect~\cite{harvey2016cross}, and validation performance is likely to overstate test performance.

\subsection{Stage III: Dynamic Routing}
\label{sec:earnhft_stage3}

The minute-level router is another target-network value learner. The published method calls it DDQN~\cite{van2016deep}, but the released update, like the low-level update, takes the next-action maximum directly over the target network and is therefore DQN-style rather than strict Double DQN. Acting once per minute reduces the number of decisions by $59/60=98.33\%$ relative to a second-level controller. At each step the router is restricted to specialists whose initial-position index matches the current position, leaving one candidate per trend label. No hindsight teacher is built at this level: the horizon is already reduced, and each high-level action invokes an entire specialist rather than choosing a target position.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:earnhft_setup}

\paragraph{Datasets.} We evaluate on four cryptocurrencies covering mainstream and niche assets and bull, bear, and sideways conditions, summarised in \tref{tab:earnhft_dataset}. All four are spot trading pairs on Binance, from which the second-level OHLC and LOB snapshots derive. On each, the last nine days are used for testing, the preceding nine for validation, and the remainder for training. Low-level agents are trained on the training set; the validation set is segmented and labelled for model selection; the router is then trained on the training set and selected on the same validation interval before testing. The released preprocessing uses five LOB levels for state-feature construction, resamples the merged stream to one-second frequency, and forward-fills missing rows. It does not state the timestamp timezone, and the repository omits the selected-feature \texttt{.npy} artefacts loaded by the training programs, so the exact 54- and 19-feature lists cannot be reconstructed from the release alone.

\paragraph{Metrics.} We report the six financial metrics defined in \sref{sec:bg_metrics}: TR (profit); ASR, ACR, ASoR (risk-adjusted profit); and AVOL, MDD (risk). \paragraph{Baselines.} We compare against six methods. The policy-based RL baselines are PPO~\cite{schulman2017proximal} and DRA~\cite{briola2021deep}; the value-based RL baselines are DQN~\cite{mnih2015human} and CDQNRP~\cite{zhu2022quantitative}; and the rule-based methods are MACD~\cite{appel2005technical} and IV~\cite{chordia2002order}. The released low-level configuration uses Adam, a replay capacity of $10^{6}$, minibatches of 512, reward scale 30, a learning rate decaying from $10^{-2}$ to $5\times10^{-4}$, $\alpha_{\mathrm{KL}}$ decaying from 256 to 128, and Polyak coefficient $\tau_{\mathrm{poly}}=0.005$. Both levels use $\gamma_l=\gamma_h=1$. Each EarnHFT configuration and each learned baseline is trained once with fixed seed 12345; the 50 checkpoints are not seed replications. The maximum holding is $0.01$ BTC on the two Bitcoin pairs, $0.1$ ETH, and $4{,}000$ GALA. The published study reports approximately ten hours for the full experiment set.

\subsection{Trading Behaviour and Computational Cost}
\label{sec:earnhft_behaviour}

The reported trajectories show different position patterns across markets. BTCT and BTCU contain relatively few target-position changes; ETH contains more frequent changes; and GALA contains longer holding intervals. BTCU includes the $0.015\%$ commission whereas BTCT is commission-free, so their trading counts are not directly comparable on price dynamics alone. The ETH and GALA patterns are consistent with their labelled bear and bull periods, respectively, but the observational plots do not identify why the networks chose each action or establish that they learned a general regime detector.

\paragraph{Computational cost.} The published study reports approximately ten hours on an RTX~4090. This covers constructing the teacher tables, running four preference-conditioned trainings, saving 200 checkpoints, segmenting the validation paths, selecting the pools, and training the routers. At evaluation time the model computation is one specialist-network forward pass per second and one router-network pass per minute. No end-to-end latency benchmark, exchange connectivity, or live order-execution timing is reported, so this computational count is not a live-trading latency claim.

\section{Chapter Summary}
\label{sec:earnhft_summary}

This chapter presented EarnHFT as a three-stage hierarchical RL method for second-level target-position control in historical order-book replay. A sample-path dynamic-programming table supplies teacher-to-student softmax-KL supervision and hindsight-greedy demonstrations; preference-conditioned checkpoints provide a specialist pool; and a minute-level target-network router switches among the retained specialists. The discounted tabular surrogate in Theorem~\ref{thm:earnhft_convergence} motivates centred-value supervision, but the reported function-approximation experiments use a different KL objective and $\gamma_l=\gamma_h=1$, so the theorem is not a convergence guarantee for them. On the four single-seed, nine-day test paths, EarnHFT has the largest tabled TR on all four and the largest risk-adjusted metrics on three; the ablations on ETH and GALA associate both teacher components with higher reported reward sums. In the language of the thesis, EarnHFT is the first instantiation of the decompose--specialise--aggregate pattern, with a value-based router selecting one specialist at a time. The next chapter retains the pattern but considers leveraged futures, where aggregation must additionally manage risk and distributional familiarity.
