\section[Quantitative Trading and Reinforcement Learning]{Quantitative Trading and\\Reinforcement Learning}
\label{sec:intro_qt_rl}

Quantitative trading is the discipline of converting market data into trading decisions through explicit, data-driven rules. A quantitative strategy ingests a stream of prices, volumes, and order-flow signals, and at each decision point it must choose what to hold and how to adjust that holding. Stated this way, quantitative trading is, at its core, a problem of \emph{sequential decision-making under uncertainty}: the present action changes the portfolio, the portfolio interacts with a stochastic, non-stationary market---one often informally described as adversarial, although throughout this thesis the market is modelled as an exogenous process replayed from historical data---and the consequences are realised only over a horizon of subsequent steps. The natural formal language for such problems is reinforcement learning (RL), in which an agent learns a policy that maps states to actions so as to maximise a cumulative reward~\cite{sutton2018reinforcement}.

Reinforcement learning is attractive for trading for reasons that distinguish it from a one-step predictive pipeline. The supervised baselines used in this thesis predict a future return or direction and optimise a predictive loss; sophisticated supervised time-series models need not assume independent and identically distributed (IID) data, but their loss still does not by itself specify how predictions become positions under costs and constraints. Transaction costs and inventory make the payoff of an action depend on the current position, coupling consecutive decisions. Reinforcement learning offers one way to model that path dependence and optimise a trajectory return directly, although it is not automatically preferable to a well-designed predictive strategy. Researchers have applied it across the quantitative-trading stack, from the direct-reinforcement systems of Moody and Saffell~\cite{moody2001learning} through order execution~\cite{nevmyvaka2006reinforcement}, intraday trading~\cite{sun2022deepscalper,briola2021deep,zhu2022quantitative}, and portfolio management~\cite{jiang2017deep}.

The premise of this thesis is therefore that reinforcement learning is a useful framework for the sequential trading problems studied here; its subject is the gap between that premise and practice. Financial markets violate, sometimes severely, the assumptions on which standard RL algorithms were designed and validated. The contribution of this thesis is to isolate, for several distinct and economically important trading tasks, the obstacle treated as decisive in each stated setting and to design a method that mitigates or works around it, rather than claiming one remedy for all financial reinforcement learning.

\section[Why Reinforcement Learning is Hard in Financial Markets]{Why Reinforcement Learning is Hard in\\Financial Markets}
\label{sec:intro_challenges}

The difficulties are clearest when each property of the data-generating and trading process is connected to its consequence for learning. The technical chapters then address different parts of this chain rather than three interchangeable versions of the same problem.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Distribution drift and finite history.} Relationships between features, actions, and returns change as market conditions change, while only a finite historical window is available for fitting. The immediate learning consequence is train--test mismatch: arguments derived for stationary Markov decision processes (MDPs) need not transfer to future periods, and model or selection complexity can turn repeated backtesting into overfitting~\cite{harvey2016cross}.

\item \textbf{Weak feedback over long horizons.} The predictable component of returns is small relative to price noise, and at second resolution an evaluation period can contain millions of decisions. The resulting reward signal is noisy and temporally diffuse, which lengthens credit assignment and makes training and evaluation expensive. Long-horizon financial RL also faces the out-of-sample generalisation difficulty documented in trade execution~\cite{zhang2023towards}.

\item \textbf{Path-dependent and asymmetric payoffs.} Positions, transaction costs, funding, and margin rules couple successive decisions. Leverage does not make the underlying price process intrinsically more uncertain, but it magnifies profit-and-loss variation and makes errors asymmetric: an unfamiliar state can lead to liquidation before an expected-return learner recovers. The learning problem therefore requires risk controls and careful account-state modelling, not only a larger return objective.

\item \textbf{Endogenous learning targets.} When a candidate is rewarded by its marginal contribution to an evolving set of strategies or signals, changing that set changes the reward attached to the same candidate. This creates objective drift inside the algorithm even if the historical data window is held fixed, complicating optimisation and post-selection analysis.
\end{itemize}

No single method removes all of these obstacles at once, nor should one expect it to: the obstacles interact, and the binding constraint differs by task. This thesis instead studies three concrete tasks, isolates the obstacle that is decisive for each, and addresses it.

\paragraph{Task I: Second-level target-position control (\cref{ch:earnhft}).} The first task is second-level target-position control of a single cryptocurrency in historical order-book replay, referred to in this thesis as high-frequency trading (HFT). The agent repeatedly adjusts its position to profit from short-lived price fluctuations. Two obstacles dominate: the decision horizon contains millions of steps, and one policy must span changing trends. Within the released replay model, future prices are exogenous to the agent because the simulator has no market-impact response. EarnHFT therefore computes a sample-path optimal Q-table by dynamic programming and uses its action distribution to supervise the second-level learner. It then trains a pool of trend-conditioned agents and an offline minute-level router that is frozen for deployment. Across the four dense second-level test paths, EarnHFT attains the highest total return on each dataset. This protocol does not model latency, queue priority, or endogenous market impact and therefore does not evaluate a live order-execution system.

\paragraph{Task II: Leveraged futures (\cref{ch:fineft}).} The second task moves from spot to leveraged futures, introducing long and short positions, funding, margin constraints, and high leverage. FineFT uses selective updates to produce differentiated ensemble members, then fits one variational autoencoder to the states associated with each inferred dynamic. A rule-based router uses the resulting familiarity scores to select a learner or a conservative fallback. Across the four markets, FineFT has the highest average total return among the learned methods and the lowest average maximum drawdown; the routing ablation further shows the benefit of the VAE-based router and its risk threshold.

\paragraph{Task III: Formulaic alpha mining (\cref{ch:alphaqd}).} The third task no longer chooses positions; it generates short symbolic predictive signals, or ``alphas'', for a downstream portfolio. Pool-based methods may reward a new formula by its marginal contribution to an evolving pool, so the same formula's target can change as the pool changes. AlphaQD uses a candidate-only training-window reward and a separate MAP-Elites archive. The method is evaluated on Chinese A-share and U.S.\ panels, with its strongest portfolio result on A-shares.

\section{A Shared Design Perspective}
\label{sec:intro_pattern}

Although the three studies were motivated by different tasks and developed independently, comparing them reveals a useful design perspective. It is deliberately broader than a common mathematical model:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Structure} the relevant heterogeneity: market trends in EarnHFT, transition subsets inferred from ensemble TD errors in FineFT, and return-source behaviours in AlphaQD.
\item \textbf{Construct differentiated candidates}: trading policies in the first two studies and elite formulas in the third.
\item \textbf{Aggregate} those candidates through a task-appropriate mechanism: routing to one policy or combining several signals.
\end{enumerate}

This comparison does not imply that AlphaQD partitions a non-stationary market, that an elite formula is an RL specialist, or that each region is stationary. The narrower connective thread concerns where aggregation information enters learning. EarnHFT trains its router after freezing the specialists; FineFT uses a non-learned rule over reconstruction scores; AlphaQD keeps archive membership out of the generator's reward. Joint learning can be appropriate in other settings, so the thesis advances the following as a falsifiable working hypothesis rather than a principle established by these three non-controlled studies:
\begin{quote}
\itshape When aggregation would make a candidate's target change as other candidates enter or leave the set, separating the aggregation criterion from that candidate's learning reward can improve target stability and make selection assumptions easier to state.
\end{quote}

\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:intro_contributions}

The contributions of this thesis are as follows.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{EarnHFT} (\cref{ch:earnhft}): a hierarchical reinforcement-learning framework for single-asset high-frequency position control. Its technical core is a sample-path optimal Q-teacher computed by dynamic programming under exogenous historical replay; together with trend-conditioned training and an offline minute-level router, it attains the highest total return in each of the four reported datasets.

\item \textbf{FineFT} (\cref{ch:fineft}): a risk-aware ensemble framework for leveraged futures. It introduces a selective-update mechanism driven by an ensemble TD matrix and uses per-dynamic VAE reconstruction losses as heuristic distributional-familiarity scores for rule-based routing; these scores are not calibrated probabilities of policy failure. Across the four reported markets, it has the highest average total return among learned methods and the lowest unfiltered average maximum drawdown.

\item \textbf{AlphaQD} (\cref{ch:alphaqd}): a quality-diversity reinforcement-learning method for formulaic alpha mining. It separates a candidate-only GFlowNet reward from a MAP-Elites archive keyed on a return-source descriptor. The chapter develops analytical motivations under explicitly conditional or idealised premises: an abstract equal-weight ICIR bound and a finite-class selection-complexity comparison conditional on a realised archive. Neither result is a finite-sample guarantee for the fitted ridge portfolio or for the complete data-dependent pipeline.

\item \textbf{A comparative perspective} (\cref{ch:introduction}, \cref{ch:conclusion}): the observation that all three methods structure heterogeneity, construct differentiated candidates, and aggregate them, together with the narrower working hypothesis that aggregation information should be separated from a candidate's reward when it would otherwise make that target evolve.
\end{itemize}

\section{Organisation of the Thesis}
\label{sec:intro_outline}

The thesis is organised into four parts. \textbf{Part~I} (Foundations) comprises this introduction, \cref{ch:preliminaries}, which establishes the shared financial and reinforcement-learning background of the technical chapters, and \cref{ch:literature_review}, which surveys related work organised by task and synthesises the open challenges that motivate them. \textbf{Part~II} (Reinforcement Learning for Trading) contains the two trading studies: \cref{ch:earnhft} presents EarnHFT for high-frequency trading, and \cref{ch:fineft} presents FineFT for leveraged futures. \textbf{Part~III} (Reinforcement Learning for Signal Generation) contains \cref{ch:alphaqd}, which presents AlphaQD for formulaic alpha mining. \textbf{Part~IV} (Epilogue) consists of~\cref{ch:conclusion}, which revisits the unifying perspective, states the limitations of each study, and outlines directions for future work. Supporting proofs and extended derivations are presented within the technical chapters in which they arise.
