\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:fineft_intro}

Futures are standardised contracts obligating the exchange of an asset at a predetermined date and price. The instruments traded in this chapter are \emph{perpetual} futures, a variant with no delivery date whose price is kept aligned with the spot price through a periodic funding-fee mechanism. Futures account for the majority of cryptocurrency trading volume~\cite{aleti2021bitcoin} because they permit both long and short positions and offer high leverage, attracting risk-seeking traders and creating a deeply liquid market. Futures trading is therefore a crucial domain for quantitative methods, and the natural next task after the spot high-frequency trading of \cref{ch:earnhft}.

Reinforcement learning has succeeded across many trading tasks~\cite{qin2024earnhft,zong2024macrohft}, but those methods target low-leverage markets such as spot or stock and do not transfer to leveraged futures, for two reasons that constitute Gap~II of \sref{sec:bg_gaps} and are illustrated in \fref{fig:fineft_motivation}.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Leverage amplifies the stochasticity of the reward.} High leverage magnifies the inherent noise of financial markets~\cite{sun2023mastering}: even in nearly identical states, identical actions can lead to vastly different rewards and next states. This aleatoric uncertainty, illustrated in \fref{fig:fineft_motivation}(a) by the wide variation in future return among states that lie close in a one-dimensional embedding, undermines the convergence and stability of RL and reduces data efficiency.
\item \textbf{Agents are unaware of their capability boundary.} Because RL overfits the training set, a policy becomes unreliable on market states unseen during training, exposing it to severe capital loss~\cite{lan2022generalization}. A black-swan event---such as the late-2022 shift that coincided with aggressive interest-rate hikes, illustrated in \fref{fig:fineft_motivation}(b) by an abrupt relocation of a state cluster in the embedding---can liquidate an agent that lacks self-awareness of its competence. This is an \emph{epistemic} uncertainty that low-leverage methods can afford to ignore but leveraged ones cannot.
\end{itemize}

This chapter presents FineFT, an e\textbf{F}ficient and r\textbf{I}sk-aware e\textbf{N}semble r\textbf{E}inforcement learning method for \textbf{F}utures \textbf{T}rading, with three stages summarised in \fref{fig:fineft_pipeline}. It follows the same decompose--specialise--aggregate pattern as EarnHFT, but the aggregation mechanism is now risk-aware and the agent is made competence-aware.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Stage~I (selective-update ensemble).} We initialise $N$ deep Q-networks, pre-train them on demonstrations, and then update them \emph{selectively}: for each transition we update only the learner with the smallest temporal-difference error and its neighbours. This trains diverse specialists efficiently and segments the market automatically.
\item \textbf{Stage~II (filter and boundary).} We filter the ensemble by profitability under each market dynamic to shrink the pool, and train a variational autoencoder per dynamic to capture each specialist's capability boundary and detect out-of-distribution states.
\item \textbf{Stage~III (risk-aware routing).} Guided by VAE losses on recent states, we route to the best in-distribution specialist for profit, and fall back to a conservative policy on out-of-distribution states to control risk.
\end{enumerate}

On crypto futures with $5\times$ leverage, FineFT is evaluated against twelve state-of-the-art baselines on six metrics. Averaged across the four markets, FineFT reduces maximum drawdown by roughly 63\% relative to the runner-up, Winnow, once baselines with negligible market exposure (e.g., RAQR) are excluded from the drawdown comparison, while attaining the highest average total return among the learned methods, although it is not uniformly dominant on every individual market.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:fineft_formulation}

Beyond the concepts of \sref{sec:bg_instruments}, futures trading introduces \emph{leverage} $L_t$, the ratio of position value to required margin; the \emph{mark price} $M_t$; a \emph{market order loss} $O_t$ that captures slippage and commission; a \emph{funding fee} $F_{ft}=F_{rt}M_tH_t$, settled every four or eight hours (depending on the pair) on the mark-price notional $M_tH_t$, where the funding rate $F_{rt}$ is set by the exchange from the spot--perpetual price gap and the signed position $H_t$ fixes the direction of the payment: when $F_{rt}$ is positive, long positions pay short positions, and when it is negative, short positions pay long positions; and the \emph{margin balance} $V_t$, the sum of cash (wallet balance) and position value, where the realised costs---commission, slippage, and funding fees---are deducted from the wallet balance. The objective is to maximise the margin balance via leveraged market orders on a single asset at a five-minute decision interval.

\subsection{A Dynamic Parametric MDP}
\label{sec:fineft_dpmdp}

To make the non-stationarity explicit, we model futures trading as a dynamic parametric MDP (DP-MDP)~\cite{xie2020deep}, the tuple $(\mathcal{S},\mathcal{A},P,r,\gamma,T,Z,P_z)$ that augments the MDP of \sref{sec:bg_mdp} with a \emph{dynamic} space $Z$ and its transition $P_z$. The dynamic $z$ governs the transition $P:\mathcal{S}\times Z\times\mathcal{A}\times\mathcal{S}\to[0,1]$ but is not revealed to the agent, and it evolves much more slowly than the state. Crucially, this means that \emph{over a short horizon the experienced transitions behave as those of a stationary MDP}---the assumption that legitimises training a specialist per dynamic.

The goal is to learn a set of specialist policies $\Pi_A=(\pi_{\theta_1},\dots,\pi_{\theta_m})$ and a router $\pi_{\theta_r}:\mathcal{S}\to Z$ that compose an optimal strategy $\pi^\star=\argmax_{A,\theta_r}\mathbb{E}_{\Pi_A,\pi_{\theta_r}}[\sum_{t=0}^T\gamma^t r_t]$. The state $S_t$ consists of around 300 technical indicators $y_t$ and the position $H_t$; the reward is the margin-balance change
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fineft_reward}
r_t = V_{t+1}-V_t = H_t(M_{t+1}-M_t)-O_t,
\end{equation}
combining valuation change and order loss. Funding fees are charged by the simulator at their four- or eight-hourly settlement timestamps and deducted from the wallet balance, and therefore from $V_t$; the expansion in \eqref{eq:fineft_reward} is exact between settlements, with the fee $F_{ft}$ additionally subtracted at settlement steps. The action is a target (position, leverage) pair drawn from a finite pool, executed instantly by a market order.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:fineft_method}

\subsection{Stage I: Efficient Ensemble with Selective Update}
\label{sec:fineft_stage1}

A mixture of experts performs well on trading tasks but is expensive to construct, because every specialist is trained independently~\cite{qin2024earnhft,sun2023mastering}. FineFT instead trains the ensemble jointly: all learners share a pre-training phase that installs common knowledge, after which each transition updates only the best-fitting learner and its neighbours. The aim is an ensemble in which each learner masters one dynamic $z$ of the DP-MDP.

Each of the $N$ learners is a multilayer perceptron with identical architecture but a distinct random seed, assigned an index $1,\dots,N$.

\paragraph{Pre-training with demonstrations.} As new traders are trained together before specialising, we first train all learners on the same demonstration transitions. Following the principle of \cref{ch:earnhft}, we build a \emph{constrained} optimal action value (Algorithm~\ref{alg:fineft_coav}) by adding a liquidation mask to the dynamic-programming construction, and use it both as an optimal actor (to generate demonstrations) and an optimal value supervisor. The mask follows the exchange's actual liquidation rule: a position is liquidated when the margin balance $V_t$ falls to the maintenance margin $M_m=k\,\mathrm{NPV}_t-j$, where $\mathrm{NPV}_t=M_t|H_t|$ is the mark-price notional of the position and the bracket constants $(k,j)$ are taken from Binance's quick-calculation table for each contract and notional tier. Because $V_t$ is path-dependent---it depends on the entry price and the realised costs accumulated so far---the mask cannot be evaluated from the table indices $(t,p,a)$ alone; it is computed by simulating the account along the replayed price path while the table is filled. Three auxiliary policies---emptying, maximally long, maximally short---add diverse transitions. Each learner is updated with a Huber TD error~\cite{huber1992robust}, robust to the outliers leverage produces, plus the KL supervision:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fineft_pretrain}
L(\theta_i) = L_{td_i} + \alpha_{\mathrm{KL}}\,\mathrm{KL}\!\left(Q(s,\cdot;\theta_i)\,\|\,Q^\star(s,\cdot)\right),
\quad
L_{td_i} = \mathcal{H}\!\left(r + \gamma\max_{a'} Q(s',a';\theta_i') - Q(s,a;\theta_i)\right),
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{H}$ is the Huber loss, quadratic for small errors and linear for large ones, and $\alpha_{\mathrm{KL}}$ is the decaying KL coefficient of \cref{ch:earnhft}. The total pre-training loss sums \eqref{eq:fineft_pretrain} over all learners.

\begin{algorithm}[!htb]
\caption{Constrained Optimal Action Value}
\label{alg:fineft_coav}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} LOB series $B$, mark-price series $M$ of length $N$, action space $A$, mask penalty $P$, capital $C$, maintenance-margin coefficients $(k,j)$ from Binance's quick-calculation table
\State \textbf{Output:} table $Q^\star$ of optimal action values
\State Initialise $Q^\star$ with shape $(N,|A|,|A|)$ and all elements $0$.
\For{$t \gets N-1$ \textbf{to} $1$}
  \For{$p,a \gets 1$ \textbf{to} $|A|$}
    \State $Q^\star[t,p,a] \gets \max_{a'} Q^\star[t+1,a,a'] + r_t$ \Comment{$r_t$ via \eqref{eq:fineft_reward}}
    \State $M_m \gets k\,\mathrm{NPV}_t - j$ \Comment{maintenance margin at the current notional}
    \If{$V_t \le M_m$}
      \State $Q^\star[t,p,a] \gets Q^\star[t,p,a] - P$ \Comment{liquidation mask; $V_t$ simulated along the replayed path}
    \EndIf
  \EndFor
\EndFor
\State \textbf{return} $Q^\star$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Selective update with neighbours.} Although dynamics share basic principles, their most profitable strategies conflict; diversity therefore matters. Prior work obtains diversity by training learners independently with preference sampling~\cite{qin2024earnhft}, at high cost. FineFT instead shares transitions selectively. For each transition we identify the learner with the smallest TD error and assign larger update weight to that learner and to its index-neighbours. The smallest TD error is used as a \emph{heuristic compatibility score}: a small TD residual does not in general certify the most accurate value estimate, but it is cheap to compute and indicates that the transition is consistent with the learner's current estimates. Index adjacency likewise carries no market semantics of its own---the learner ordering is arbitrary---so sharing updates with index-neighbours is a heuristic coupling whose practical effect is to allocate a small group of learners, rather than a single one, to each dynamic. Learners that fit poorly on this transition are left untouched, because their competence is presumed to lie in a different dynamic that the transition would only corrupt. This is designed to create a positive feedback loop: as a learner specialises in a dynamic, its TD error there falls, so future transitions from that dynamic are increasingly routed to it, which reinforces the specialisation.

Concretely, for each transition we form an ensemble TD-error matrix $L$ with entries
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fineft_etd}
L_{ij} = \mathcal{H}\!\left(r + \gamma\max Q(s',\cdot;\theta_j') - Q(s,a;\theta_i)\right),
\end{equation}
the cross-learner Huber TD error ($L_{ii}$ is learner $i$'s own error). Algorithm~\ref{alg:fineft_weight} converts $L$ into a weight matrix $\mathcal{W}$: it locates the best learner $i^\star=\argmin_i L_{ii}$, expands a neighbourhood of radius $m$, and assigns diagonal weights that decay with distance from $i^\star$ and off-diagonal weights that decay with index difference. For an interior $i^\star$ with a full window the diagonal weights decay from one at $i^\star$ to one-half at the window edges; when $i^\star$ lies within $m$ of the ensemble boundary, the truncated window instead drives the farthest retained neighbour's weight to zero---a boundary asymmetry we keep for simplicity. In the degenerate case $i_{\max}=i_{\min}$ (radius $m=0$ or ensemble size $N=1$) the decay ratio is undefined, and the weight of $i^\star$ is set to one directly. The total selective-update loss for a transition is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fineft_final_loss}
L = \sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{j=1}^{N}\mathcal{W}_{ij}L_{ij} + \mathbf{d}_{\mathcal{W}}\,O_{\mathrm{KL}}^{\top},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{d}_{\mathcal{W}}$ is the diagonal of $\mathcal{W}$ and $O_{\mathrm{KL}}$ collects the per-learner KL supervision. The cross-learner terms share experience among learners of similar dynamics while preventing experience poisoning across dissimilar ones.

\paragraph{Heuristic rationale for the selective update.} We do not claim a convergence theorem for the selective update; what follows is a mechanism-level rationale, stated together with the assumptions it relies on. In the pre-training phase, every learner is updated with the same optimal-value supervision used by EarnHFT, now constrained by the liquidation mask of Algorithm~\ref{alg:fineft_coav}; the tabular idealisation of that update is discussed around the operator $\mathcal{T}$ of \eqref{eq:earnhft_operator} in \cref{ch:earnhft}, and the caveats attached to it there apply here as well. For the selective-update phase, consider transitions from a single dynamic $z$: by the DP-MDP assumption of \sref{sec:fineft_dpmdp} they are approximately generated by a stationary MDP $M_z$, and the update of the best-fitting learner $i^\star$ on them is a Huber-TD update under $M_z$ with a non-negative supervision term. For the intended specialisation to follow, several further assumptions are needed: (i) a \emph{fixed assignment} of learners to dynamics, so that $i^\star$ does not switch while $z$ is active; (ii) a \emph{tabular} value representation; (iii) a discount $\gamma<1$; (iv) \emph{non-overlapping TD-error rankings} across dynamics, so that transitions from $z$ are consistently routed to the same learner; and (v) a \emph{monotone decrease} of each specialist's TD error on its own dynamic, so that the positive feedback loop described above is self-reinforcing. None of these is guaranteed by the algorithm itself: the assignment $i^\star=\argmin_i L_{ii}$ can switch as the networks evolve; a small TD residual need not indicate a small value error; the Huber loss shifts the fixed point away from the optimal action value precisely under the asymmetric, heavy-tailed noise that motivates its use; the cross-learner weights of Algorithm~\ref{alg:fineft_weight} are not normalised---rows of $\mathcal{W}$ can sum to more than one, so the coupling is not a convex combination---and they pull neighbouring learners towards each other's bootstrap targets; and the KL supervision draws all learners towards the same constrained value table rather than towards per-dynamic optima. The deployed implementation, which uses function approximation, lies outside this tabular idealisation; the result should be read as motivation rather than a guarantee for the deployed algorithm.

\begin{algorithm}[!htb]
\caption{Construction of the Weight Matrix}
\label{alg:fineft_weight}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} ensemble TD-error matrix $\mathcal{L}$, neighbour count $m$
\State \textbf{Output:} weight matrix $\mathcal{W}$
\State Initialise $\mathcal{W}$ with shape $(N,N)$ and all elements $0$.
\State $i^\star \gets \argmin_i L_{ii}$; \; $i_{\min}\gets\max(i^\star-m,1)$; \; $i_{\max}\gets\min(i^\star+m,N)$
\If{$i_{\max} = i_{\min}$} \Comment{degenerate window ($m=0$ or $N=1$)}
  \State $\mathcal{W}_{i^\star i^\star} \gets 1$; \; \textbf{return} $\mathcal{W}$
\EndIf
\For{$i = i_{\min}$ \textbf{to} $i_{\max}$}
  \State $\mathcal{W}_{ii} \gets 1 - \dfrac{|i-i^\star|}{i_{\max}-i_{\min}}$ \Comment{diagonal decay}
\EndFor
\For{$i,j \gets 1$ \textbf{to} $N$}
  \If{$i \neq j$}
    \State $\mathcal{W}_{ij} \gets \min(\mathcal{W}_{ii},\mathcal{W}_{jj})\left(1 - \dfrac{|i-j|}{i_{\max}-i_{\min}}\right)$ \Comment{off-diagonal decay}
  \EndIf
\EndFor
\State \textbf{return} $\mathcal{W}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Stage II: Ensemble Filter and Boundary Identification}
\label{sec:fineft_stage2}

Knowing which dynamic each learner suits is necessary but not sufficient for safe routing; we must also know the boundary within which each learner is competent.

\paragraph{Filtering by profitability.} We divide the market into dynamics by slope, following \cref{ch:earnhft}: the series is low-pass filtered, segmented at local extrema, and adjacent chunks with similar slope are merged until stable, yielding $m$ dynamics. We back-test each learner across initial positions and retain, for each dynamic, the learner with the highest average profitability, producing a small, diverse, profitable pool.

\paragraph{Capability boundary.} We define the \emph{capability boundary} of the specialist retained for dynamic $i$ as the set of market-state representations on which that specialist was effectively trained---operationally, the support of the per-dynamic state distribution as estimated by a generative model. A state inside the boundary is one the model can reconstruct well; a state outside it is, by the same model, out of distribution. Reporting the boundary explicitly converts the agent's own competence from an implicit assumption of the training set into an estimable quantity at deployment, and it is the object the variational autoencoders of the next paragraph model. The boundary is established once on the training and validation windows and is held fixed at test time: the risk threshold of stage III is tuned on the validation window---never on the test window---so the deployment policy makes no use of test-time hindsight. One caveat tempers this construction: reconstruction quality is a property of the state distribution, not of the policy. A state the generative model reconstructs well is one that was frequently seen, which does not by itself guarantee that the retained specialist performs well there; we do not validate the correlation between the model's scores and realised policy losses on held-out data, so the boundary should be read as a distributional proxy for competence rather than a calibrated competence estimate.

\paragraph{Boundary identification with VAEs.} To capture each learner's capability boundary we train a variational autoencoder~\cite{kingma2013auto} on the market-state representations of each dynamic. A VAE encodes its input into a regularised latent distribution and decodes a reconstruction; because it gives a probabilistic measure of how well a new input matches the training distribution, it provides a reconstruction-based out-of-distribution score. For $m$ dynamics we train $m$ VAEs, each on the states $Y_i$ of its dynamic, minimising
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fineft_vae}
L^i(y\in Y_i) = N_{LL}\!\left(\mu_d, \tfrac{1}{2}\log\sigma_d^2, y\right) + \mathrm{KLD}\!\left(\mu,\log\sigma^2\right),
\end{equation}
the reconstruction negative log-likelihood plus the normal-distribution regulariser. Here $\mu_d$ and $\sigma_d^2$ are the decoder's output mean and variance, $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$ are the encoder's output mean and variance, $N_{LL}\!\left(\mu_d,\tfrac{1}{2}\log\sigma_d^2,y\right)=\tfrac{(y-\mu_d)^2}{2\sigma_d^2}+\tfrac{1}{2}\log\sigma_d^2+\tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi)$ is the Gaussian negative log-likelihood of the input $y$ under the decoder output, and $\mathrm{KLD}\!\left(\mu,\log\sigma^2\right)=\mathrm{KL}\!\left(\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)\,\|\,\mathcal{N}(0,I)\right)$ is the standard normal regulariser, written with the $\mathrm{KL}(\cdot\,\|\,\cdot)$ notation of the symbol table. After training we record, for each VAE $M^{v_i}$, the negative loss on every state of its dynamic as reference scores $R^i$, used at deployment.

\subsection{Stage III: Risk-Aware Heuristic Routing}
\label{sec:fineft_stage3}

\paragraph{Conservative policy.} For states never seen during training or validation we design a conservative policy: hold the position while the single-trade drawdown stays under 5\%, and close it once that threshold is breached. The policy only closes, never opens, positions, which reduces position churn. Note that it does not exit immediately on unfamiliar states: a leveraged position is held until the drawdown threshold triggers, so losses up to that threshold can still be incurred before the stop-loss cuts them.

\paragraph{Routing with VAE losses.} Training a high-level RL router is data-inefficient~\cite{qin2024earnhft,zong2024macrohft}, so we route heuristically. Given a window of recent states $Y^u_T=(y_{T-u},\dots,y_T)$, each state's score under dynamic $i$ is its empirical-CDF position among the reference scores $R^i$, smoothed by an exponentially discounted score:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fineft_ema}
R^i_t = F_{R^i}\!\left(-L^i(y_t)\right),
\quad
Re^{iu}_{T_t} = \rho\,Re^{iu}_{T_{t-1}} + R^{iu}_t,
\quad
Re^{iu}_T = Re^{iu}_{T_T},
\end{equation}
where $R^{iu}_t$ denotes the score $R^i_t$ of the state $y_t$ within the window $Y^u_T$, $\rho\in(0,1)$ is the decay coefficient, and the recursion is initialised at $Re^{iu}_{T_{T-u}}=R^i_{T-u}$. The recursion is an \emph{unnormalised} exponentially discounted sum rather than a normalised moving average: with scores of comparable magnitude its steady-state scale is $1/(1-\rho)$ times that of a single score. The tuned hyperparameters, however, pair decay coefficients of $0.94$--$0.99$ across the four markets (steady-state factors of roughly $17$--$100$) with risk thresholds of $0.37$--$0.76$, which lie on the scale of a \emph{single} ECDF score; the recursion as written and the tuned thresholds are therefore not expressed on a common scale. Two readings would reconcile them---either the recent ECDF scores are typically near zero under distribution shift, so the discounted sum stays far below its steady-state scale, or the implementation rescales the score before the threshold comparison---but the available description does not settle which holds, and we record this scale inconsistency as a limitation to be verified against the code. We select the dynamic $i=\argmax_i Re^{iu}_T$ and compare its score with a risk threshold $\tau_{\mathrm{risk}}$: if it falls below $\tau_{\mathrm{risk}}$, the recent states are unfamiliar and we choose the conservative policy; otherwise we deploy the corresponding specialist (Algorithm~\ref{alg:fineft_routing}). One caveat: the routing takes the maximum of $m$ per-dynamic scores, while $\tau_{\mathrm{risk}}$ is calibrated against individual score distributions rather than against the distribution of their maximum; taking the maximum inflates the accepted score, which systematically increases the chance of accepting an out-of-distribution window as familiar. Recalibrating the threshold on the max-score distribution would remove this bias; we note it as a limitation. This is the second instantiation of the thesis's aggregation mechanism---no longer a learned router, but a heuristic driven by generative uncertainty estimates, and hence partly decoupled from the policy learning.

\begin{algorithm}[!htb]
\caption{Risk-Aware Heuristic Routing}
\label{alg:fineft_routing}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} specialists $\Pi=(\pi^1,\dots,\pi^m)$, VAEs $M^v$, reference scores $R$, state window $Y^u_T$, decay $\rho$, threshold $\tau_{\mathrm{risk}}$
\State \textbf{Output:} current strategy $\pi$
\For{dynamic $i = 1$ \textbf{to} $m$}
  \State Obtain state scores $R^i_t$ from $M^{v_i}$, $Y^u_T$, and $R^i$.
  \State Compute the exponentially discounted score $Re^{iu}_T$ by \eqref{eq:fineft_ema}.
\EndFor
\State $i \gets \argmax_i Re^{iu}_T$
\If{$Re^{iu}_T \le \tau_{\mathrm{risk}}$}
  \State \textbf{return} conservative policy $\pi^c$ \Comment{out-of-distribution}
\Else
  \State \textbf{return} specialist $\pi^i$
\EndIf
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:fineft_setup}

\paragraph{Datasets.} We evaluate on four mainstream cryptocurrencies over more than two years, covering bull, bear, and sideways conditions (\tref{tab:fineft_dataset}). On each, the last six months are used for testing, the preceding six for validation, and the first year for training. The roughly 300 state features are produced by an XGBoost-based selection: for each target variable---forward return or realised volatility over horizons from five minutes to 24 hours---an XGBoost regressor is trained on the candidate features; features with importance above $0.01$ for some target are retained; and for any pair of retained features whose absolute Pearson correlation exceeds $0.7$, only the more important one is kept. The available description does not state on which data split these importance and correlation statistics are computed; if any were computed beyond the training window, the preprocessing would leak information across splits, and we flag the absence of an explicit training-only guarantee as a limitation. The ensemble is trained on the training set; the validation set is segmented and labelled to filter the ensemble and train the VAEs, and to tune the routing hyperparameters (threshold $\tau_{\mathrm{risk}}$, decay $\rho$, window length $u$). We also note that the validation window is thereby reused many times---for ensemble filtering, VAE fitting, the ECDF reference scores, and hyperparameter tuning---so validation performance is not an unbiased estimate of generalisation, and some selection overfitting to the validation window cannot be ruled out.

\paragraph{Baselines.} We compare against twelve methods: two policy-based (PPO~\cite{schulman2017proximal}, and DRA, an LSTM-based PPO variant~\cite{briola2021deep}); two value-based (DQN~\cite{mnih2015human}, and CDQNRP~\cite{zhu2022quantitative}, the same method that appears under this name in \cref{ch:earnhft}); two rule-based (MACD~\cite{appel2005technical}, IV~\cite{chordia2002order}); two ensemble (EDQN, an ensemble DQN in the style of Averaged-DQN~\cite{anschel2017averaged}, and SUNRISE~\cite{lee2021sunrise}); one distributional (RAQR, a risk-averse quantile-regression DQN~\cite{dabney2018distributional}); and three hierarchical or routing-based (Winnow, an online expert-weighting algorithm~\cite{littlestone1988winnow}; EHFT, i.e., EarnHFT, our method from \cref{ch:earnhft}~\cite{qin2024earnhft}; and MHFT, i.e., MacroHFT~\cite{zong2024macrohft}). The same hyperparameters used by FineFT are applied to the baselines where applicable.

\paragraph{Training.} Experiments run on four RTX~4090 GPUs, completing in about six hours. The transaction cost is $0.02\%$, following Binance, with a leverage factor of $5$. We report the six metrics of \sref{sec:bg_metrics}.

\subsection{Comparison with Baselines}
\label{sec:fineft_comparison}

\tref{tab:fineft_main} reports the main comparison. FineFT attains the highest risk-adjusted profit (ASR, ACR, ASoR) on three of the four markets, and the lowest MDD once baselines with negligible exposure (e.g., RAQR) are excluded from the drawdown comparison. Several patterns are visible. Value-based methods (DQN, CDQNRP) perform well only when the trend is stable and the validation--test gap is small. CDQNRP outperforms DQN, confirming the value of training stability under high stochasticity. Policy-based methods (DRA, PPO) generally beat DQN but fail to surpass CDQNRP, which suggests premature convergence to suboptimal policies in non-stationary environments. The small gap between DRA and PPO also indicates that an LSTM encoder helps little under such noise. Rule-based methods depend heavily on the validation--test similarity that fixes their hyperparameters. Ensemble (EDQN, SUNRISE) and distributional (RAQR) methods reduce variance, and so reduce risk. RAQR does so, however, by trading almost nothing: its near-zero volatility on DOT and ETH reflects negligible participation rather than skilful control, which is why we exclude it from the drawdown comparison on those markets. We acknowledge that this exclusion rests on a qualitative judgement: exposure statistics (active fraction, average exposure, trade count, turnover) are not reported in this chapter, and a quantitative exclusion criterion would require them---a limitation of the presentation. Hierarchical and routing-based methods (Winnow, EHFT, MHFT) achieve markedly higher profit and depend less on validation--test similarity, but exhibit large drawdowns precisely because they lack any awareness of their capability boundary. FineFT retains hierarchical-level profitability on familiar states while turning conservative on unfamiliar ones, thereby avoiding the large losses the other hierarchical methods incur.

\subsection{Effectiveness of Risk-Aware Routing and Case Studies}
\label{sec:fineft_ablation_routing}

The risk-aware router is the main source of FineFT's risk reduction. \fref{fig:fineft_routing} shows how the routing decisions evolve on BTC over a window spanning the end of the validation period and the first months of the test period: the conservative policy dominates throughout, and its share rises as the market drifts further from the training window; on this market, FineFT outperforms both its no-risk-threshold variant and any single specialist. Two case studies make the mechanism concrete. First, \fref{fig:fineft_update} visualises the relationship between the updated learner index and the market dynamic during selective update (in a seven-agent diagnostic run; see the caption): distinct learners specialise in distinct dynamics---some in sudden surges, others in sharp drops, others in minor fluctuations---suggesting that the ensemble does not learn randomly but partitions the market. Second, \fref{fig:fineft_vae} shows a case in which FineFT proactively closed a BNB position upon encountering unfamiliar states---the exit was triggered by a large mean VAE loss over a rolling window, with no closing signal from the selected specialist---avoiding the loss from a subsequent decline that coincided with an on-chain upgrade event. We are explicit about the evidential status of this section: the routing ablation is reported for a single market (BTC), and the VAE-triggered exit is a single case study; the chapter does not report a four-market numerical ablation of the VAE router (with and without the risk threshold), so the router's causal contribution to the aggregate results of \tref{tab:fineft_main} rests on case-study evidence rather than on a complete controlled comparison---a limitation we acknowledge.

The pool size shows a diversification-versus-specialisation trade-off. Three agents leave too few specialists for the market dynamics observed and underperform the canonical five; seven agents over-fragment the training data per learner and the gain in specialisation does not compensate. The drawdown threshold is, by design, a risk-preference dial: a tight $1\%$ cap delivers the lowest maximum drawdown but cuts return, while a loose $10\%$ cap recovers some return at the cost of much higher drawdown. Given the post-hoc, single-market nature of this grid, we draw no robustness conclusion from it.

\paragraph{Computational cost.} Training the full FineFT pipeline---selective-update ensemble pre-training, per-dynamic VAE fitting, and validation-window hyperparameter tuning---takes approximately six hours on a server with four NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPUs and an AMD Ryzen Threadripper PRO 5995WX CPU. At inference time the cost is dominated by the joint evaluation of one DQN specialist together with the five VAEs that gate it. With PyTorch's \texttt{vmap}, joint inference completes in approximately $0.78\,\mathrm{ms}$ on a single RTX 4090, only marginally above the $0.28\,\mathrm{ms}$ of the bare DQN; both are several orders of magnitude below the five-minute decision interval, so the VAE machinery does not constrain the deployment frequency in practice.

\subsection{Generalisation to Other Asset Classes}
\label{sec:fineft_generalisation}

The main evaluation is confined to cryptocurrency futures, the setting for which FineFT was designed. To test whether the method transfers beyond crypto, we apply the same high-level framework, with an adapted environment, to two traditional financial futures with markedly different microstructure: corn futures, a commodity contract, and E-mini S\&P~500 (ES) futures, an equity-index contract. Both are sampled at daily frequency from Yahoo Finance. The crypto environment cannot transfer verbatim: its roughly 300 state features and its slippage model are built from limit-order-book data, which daily bar series do not provide, and the contract mechanics (margin schedule, funding, contract roll) differ; the state features and execution model are therefore adapted to daily bar data. The full list of environment adaptations is not documented in the source material, which we note as a limitation of this report. For each market we train on 2001--2013, validate on the following roughly five years, and test on the final roughly five years (about $3{,}000$ training and $1{,}200$ test days per market). These series are two orders of magnitude shorter than the crypto datasets---thousands of steps against about $210{,}000$ per crypto market (about $841{,}000$ in total across the four markets)---so the experiment carries less statistical power and is reported as a generalisation check rather than as a primary result.

\tref{tab:fineft_extended} compares FineFT against a $5\times$ leveraged buy-and-hold benchmark and a plain DQN on total return, Sharpe ratio, and maximum drawdown. The construction of the Buy\&Hold ($5\times$) benchmark is not documented in the source material; its reported maximum drawdowns of about $91\%$, which under real maintenance-margin rules would almost certainly have triggered liquidation (a constant $5\times$ long position would not have survived, for instance, the March 2020 decline in ES), indicate that it is most plausibly a leverage-scaled return series computed without margin or liquidation simulation. It should therefore be read as a reference scaling that is not implementable under real margin rules---a limitation we make explicit. On both markets FineFT attains the highest total return and Sharpe ratio and the lowest maximum drawdown: it cuts the maximum drawdown of leveraged buy-and-hold to roughly a third while improving total return, and it improves on every metric over the DQN. The capability-boundary machinery therefore continues to convert risk control into retained return on asset classes and data frequencies it was not tuned for, indicating that the framework is not specific to cryptocurrency.

\section{Chapter Summary}
\label{sec:fineft_summary}

This chapter presented FineFT, a three-stage risk-aware ensemble RL framework for leveraged futures. A selective-update mechanism, driven by an ensemble TD-error matrix, trains diverse specialists efficiently and segments the market automatically. A per-dynamic VAE captures each specialist's capability boundary. At deployment, the router switches between specialists and a conservative fallback by out-of-distribution detection. Averaged across the four markets, FineFT reduces maximum drawdown by roughly 63\% relative to the runner-up, Winnow, once baselines with negligible market exposure (e.g., RAQR) are excluded from the drawdown comparison, while attaining the highest average total return among the learned methods, although it is not uniformly dominant on every individual market. Relative to EarnHFT, FineFT advances the thesis on two fronts. It makes the agent aware of its competence, which addresses the epistemic uncertainty that leverage renders fatal. It also replaces the learned router with a heuristic one driven by generative uncertainty estimates, which begins to decouple aggregation from policy learning. The next chapter completes this trajectory: it moves from trading to signal generation and removes the aggregation mechanism from the learning reward altogether.
