\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:fineft_intro}

The instruments traded in this chapter are cryptocurrency perpetual futures: leveraged contracts with no delivery date whose price is kept aligned with the spot price through a periodic funding mechanism. They permit both long and short positions and provide the second trading task after the spot position-control study of \cref{ch:earnhft}.

Reinforcement learning has been used across many trading tasks~\cite{qin2024earnhft,zong2024macrohft}, but leveraged perpetual futures add account mechanics and asymmetric loss consequences that a spot-market design does not automatically address. Two motivating issues are illustrated in \fref{fig:fineft_motivation}.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Leverage magnifies reward variation.} Leverage does not change the uncertainty of the underlying market transition; it scales the resulting account gains and losses. Noisy price changes therefore produce larger-magnitude rewards and more unstable value targets, while the one-dimensional embedding in \fref{fig:fineft_motivation}(a) offers only an illustrative view of that variation.
\item \textbf{The policy has no direct signal of distributional familiarity.} A policy fitted on one historical distribution may be unreliable on unfamiliar states~\cite{lan2022generalization}, and leverage can turn such an error into liquidation. The cluster relocation in \fref{fig:fineft_motivation}(b) motivates a familiarity signal, but neither that projection nor a VAE score by itself establishes policy competence or a causal macroeconomic explanation.
\end{itemize}

The horizontal axis is time; the vertical axis is a one-dimensional t-SNE~\cite{van2008visualizing} embedding of the market state $y_t$; colour encodes the future five-minute return. \emph{(a)} Aleatoric uncertainty: state values that lie close in the embedding yield widely varying future returns. \emph{(b)} Distribution shift: a state cluster abruptly relocates during a macro event. This chapter presents FineFT, an efficient and risk-aware ensemble reinforcement-learning method for futures trading, with three stages summarised in \fref{fig:fineft_pipeline}.\footnote{This chapter has been published in the \emph{Proceedings of the 32nd ACM SIGKDD Conference on Knowledge Discovery and Data Mining (KDD'26)}.} In the comparative vocabulary of \cref{ch:introduction}, it constructs differentiated ensemble members and aggregates them with a reconstruction-score rule; the rule is intended as risk control, not calibrated self-knowledge.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Stage~I (selective-update ensemble).} We initialise $N_e$ deep Q-networks, pre-train them on demonstrations, and then update them \emph{selectively}: for each transition we update only the learner with the smallest temporal-difference error and its neighbours. This mechanism is intended to produce diverse specialists without training each network independently.
\item \textbf{Stage~II (filter and familiarity model).} We filter the ensemble by profitability under each market dynamic to shrink the pool, and train a variational autoencoder per dynamic as a proxy for distributional familiarity.
\item \textbf{Stage~III (risk-aware routing).} Guided by VAE losses on recent states, we route to the specialist with the highest familiarity score or invoke a drawdown-conditioned fallback when all scores are low.
\end{enumerate}

On crypto futures with $5\times$ leverage, FineFT is evaluated against twelve state-of-the-art baselines on six metrics. Averaged across the four markets, FineFT records an MDD of $19.46\%$, compared with $33.82\%$ for RAQR in the complete final baseline set---a reduction of $42.5\%$---while attaining the highest average total return among the learned methods. This aggregate result is not uniform across individual markets: RAQR has lower MDD on DOT and ETH, while MHFT has higher return on DOT.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:fineft_formulation}

Beyond the concepts of \sref{sec:bg_instruments}, futures trading introduces \emph{leverage} $L_t$, the ratio of position value to required margin; the \emph{mark price} $M_t$; a \emph{market order loss} $O_t$ that captures slippage and commission; a \emph{funding fee} $F_{f,t}=F_{r,t}M_tH_t$ on the signed mark-price notional, where the funding rate $F_{r,t}$ is set by the exchange from the spot--perpetual price gap; and the \emph{margin balance} $V_t=W_t+U_t$, the wallet balance $W_t$ plus unrealised profit and loss $U_t$. Commission, slippage, and funding payments are realised in the wallet. The released simulator reads the next funding-settlement timestamp from each market-data observation rather than generating settlements from a hard-coded four- or eight-hour interval. The objective is to maximise the margin balance via leveraged market orders on a single asset at a five-minute decision interval.

\subsection{A Dynamic-Parameter Markov Decision Process}
\label{sec:fineft_dpmdp}

To make the non-stationarity explicit, we model futures trading as a dynamic-parameter MDP (DP-MDP)~\cite{xie2020deep}, the tuple $(\mathcal{S},\mathcal{A},P,r,\gamma,T,Z,P_z)$ that augments the MDP of \sref{sec:bg_mdp} with a \emph{dynamic} space $Z$ and its transition $P_z$. The dynamic $z$ governs the transition $P:\mathcal{S}\times Z\times\mathcal{A}\times\mathcal{S}\to[0,1]$ but is not revealed to the agent, and it evolves much more slowly than the state. Crucially, this means that \emph{over a short horizon the experienced transitions behave as those of a stationary MDP}---the assumption that legitimises training a specialist per dynamic.

The goal is to learn a set of specialist policies $\Pi=(\pi_{\theta_1},\dots,\pi_{\theta_{n_z}})$ and router parameters $\theta_r$. Writing $\pi_{\Pi,\theta_r}$ for their composition, a horizon of $T$ transitions gives
\[
\begin{aligned}
(\Pi^\star,\theta_r^\star)
&=\argmax_{\Pi,\theta_r}
\mathbb{E}_{\pi_{\Pi,\theta_r}}
\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\gamma^t r_t\right],\\[-1pt]
\pi^\star&=\pi_{\Pi^\star,\theta_r^\star}.
\end{aligned}
\]
The state $s_t$ consists of around 300 technical indicators $y_t$ and the position $H_t$; the reward is the margin-balance change
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fineft_reward}
r_t = V_{t+1}-V_t
    = H_t(M_{t+1}-M_t)-O_t
      -\mathbb{I}^{\mathrm{fund}}_{t+1}F_{f,t+1},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbb{I}^{\mathrm{fund}}_{t+1}$ indicates a funding settlement. Thus funding enters both the account state and the learning reward, rather than affecting liquidation alone. The released online environment and the teacher utility use adjacent mark-price indices for the funding notional, an implementation-level indexing discrepancy; \eqref{eq:fineft_reward} states the accounting shared by both paths. The action is a target (position, leverage) pair drawn from a finite pool and submitted as a market order. Separately, the online simulator applies the exchange maintenance-margin rule $V_t\leq k|M_tH_t|-j$; this liquidation test is not part of the hindsight teacher described next.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:fineft_method}

\subsection{Stage I: Efficient Ensemble with Selective Update}
\label{sec:fineft_stage1}

A mixture of experts performs well on trading tasks but is expensive to construct, because every specialist is trained independently~\cite{qin2024earnhft,sun2023mastering}. FineFT instead trains the ensemble jointly: all learners share a pre-training phase that installs common knowledge, after which each transition updates only the best-fitting learner and its neighbours. The aim is an ensemble in which each learner masters one dynamic $z$ of the DP-MDP.

Each of the $N_e$ learners is a multilayer perceptron with identical architecture and a distinct random initialisation drawn after setting one fixed seed for the run; the learners are assigned indices $1,\dots,N_e$.

\paragraph{Pre-training with demonstrations.} As new traders are trained together before specialising, we first train all learners on the same demonstration transitions. The released implementation constructs the local transition table of Algorithm~\ref{alg:fineft_coav}. Its indices are time $t$, current action $p$, and next action $a$; for every $(t,p)$ pair it reinitialises a notional account with wallet $10^5$ and zero unrealised PnL. It therefore does not propagate a path-dependent margin balance through the recursion. A penalty of $10^{10}$ is assigned only when a transition reverses the sign of the position in one step or when the requested target position and leverage cannot be executed under the account and order-book constraints. This differs from the published pseudocode, which tests $V_t\leq C$: neither that capital test nor a maintenance-margin test occurs in the released teacher code. Three auxiliary policies---emptying, maximally long, and maximally short---add diverse transitions. The implementation instantiates the table twice: the value supervisor uses $\gamma=0.9$, whereas the separate table whose greedy actions form the ``perfect'' demonstration trajectory uses $\gamma=1$. The TD target also uses $\gamma=0.9$. We write the local teacher as $\widetilde Q$, reserving $Q^\star$ for an optimal action-value function. Each learner is updated with a Huber TD error~\cite{huber1992robust}, robust to the outliers leverage produces, plus KL supervision:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fineft_pretrain}
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pre}}(\theta_i)
&= \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{TD},i} + \alpha_{\mathrm{KL}}\,
\mathrm{KL}\Bigl(
  \mathrm{softmax}\bigl(\widetilde Q(s,\cdot)\bigr)
  \;\Big\|\;
  \mathrm{softmax}\bigl(Q(s,\cdot\,;\theta_i)\bigr)
\Bigr),\\[2pt]
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{TD},i}
&= \mathcal{H}\!\left(r + \gamma\max_{a'} Q(s',a';\theta_i') - Q(s,a;\theta_i)\right),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{H}$ is the Huber loss, quadratic for small errors and linear for large ones, $\theta_i'$ denotes the parameters of learner $i$'s target network, and $\alpha_{\mathrm{KL}}$ is the decaying supervision coefficient of \cref{ch:earnhft}. PyTorch's \texttt{kl\_div} call receives the student's log-probabilities as its input and the teacher distribution as its target, so the implemented direction is $\mathrm{KL}(\text{teacher}\,\|\,\text{student})$ as written above. Both action-value vectors are converted to distributions by an action-wise softmax with unit temperature; by its shift-invariance, \eqref{eq:earnhft_softmax_shift}, the term transfers action rankings while remaining blind to a common shift in value level. The total pre-training loss sums \eqref{eq:fineft_pretrain} over all learners.

\begin{algorithm}[!htb]
\caption{Local Feasible-Transition Action-Value Teacher}
\label{alg:fineft_coav}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} LOB series $B$, mark-price and funding series of length $N$, action space $A$
\Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent} infeasibility penalty $P_{\mathrm{inf}}$, discount $\gamma$
\State \textbf{Output:} local teacher table $\widetilde Q$
\State Initialise $\widetilde Q$ with shape $(N,|A|,|A|)$ and all elements $0$; slice $N-1$ is terminal.
\For{$t \gets N-2$ \textbf{down to} $0$}
  \For{$p \gets 1$ \textbf{to} $|A|$}
    \For{$a \gets 1$ \textbf{to} $|A|$}
    \State Map $p,a$ to target positions and leverages $\bigl(H(p),L(p)\bigr)$ and $\bigl(H(a),L(a)\bigr)$.
    \If{$H(p)H(a)<0$}
      \State $\widetilde Q[t,p,a] \gets -P_{\mathrm{inf}}$ \Comment{one-step sign reversal is forbidden}
    \Else
      \State Reinitialise $W\gets10^5$, $U\gets0$, and $I\gets |M_tH(p)/L(p)|$.
      \State Simulate $p\rightarrow a$ against $B_t$ to obtain the executed target $\widehat{a}$ and local reward $r_t(p,a)$.
      \If{$\widehat{a}\neq a$}
        \State $\widetilde Q[t,p,a] \gets -P_{\mathrm{inf}}$ \Comment{target is not executable}
      \Else
        \State $\widetilde Q[t,p,a] \gets r_t(p,a)+\gamma\max_{a'}\widetilde Q[t+1,a,a']$
      \EndIf
    \EndIf
    \EndFor
  \EndFor
\EndFor
\State \textbf{return} $\widetilde Q$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Selective update with neighbours.} Although dynamics share basic principles, their most profitable strategies conflict; diversity therefore matters. Prior work obtains diversity by training learners independently with preference sampling~\cite{qin2024earnhft}, at high cost. FineFT instead shares transitions selectively. For each transition we identify the learner with the smallest TD error and assign larger update weight to that learner and to its index-neighbours. The smallest TD error is used as a \emph{heuristic compatibility score}: a small TD residual does not in general certify the most accurate value estimate, but it is cheap to compute and indicates that the transition is consistent with the learner's current estimates. Index adjacency likewise carries no market semantics of its own---the learner ordering is arbitrary---so sharing updates with index-neighbours is a heuristic coupling whose practical effect is to allocate a small group of learners, rather than a single one, to each dynamic. Learners that fit poorly on this transition are left untouched, because their competence is presumed to lie in a different dynamic that the transition would only corrupt. This is designed to create a positive feedback loop: as a learner specialises in a dynamic, its TD error there falls, so future transitions from that dynamic are increasingly routed to it, which reinforces the specialisation.

Concretely, for each transition we form an ensemble TD-error matrix $\mathbf{E}$ with entries
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fineft_etd}
E_{ij} = \mathcal{H}\!\left(r + \gamma\max_{a'}Q(s',a';\theta_j') - Q(s,a;\theta_i)\right),
\end{equation}
the cross-learner Huber TD error ($E_{ii}$ is learner $i$'s own error). Algorithm~\ref{alg:fineft_weight} converts $\mathbf{E}$ into a weight matrix $\mathcal{W}$: it locates the best learner $i^\star=\argmin_i E_{ii}$, expands a neighbourhood of radius $\nu$, and assigns diagonal weights that decay with distance from $i^\star$ and off-diagonal weights that decay with index difference. For an interior $i^\star$ with a full window the diagonal weights decay from one at $i^\star$ to one-half at the window edges; when $i^\star$ lies within $\nu$ of the ensemble boundary, the truncated window instead drives the farthest retained neighbour's weight to zero---a boundary asymmetry we keep for simplicity. In the degenerate case $i_{\max}=i_{\min}$ (radius $\nu=0$ or ensemble size $N_e=1$) the decay ratio is undefined, and the weight of $i^\star$ is set to one directly. The total selective-update loss for a transition is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fineft_final_loss}
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{sel}}
&= \sum_{i=1}^{N_e}\sum_{j=1}^{N_e}\mathcal{W}_{ij}E_{ij}\\[-1pt]
&\quad+ \mathbf{d}_{\mathcal{W}}\,\mathbf{k}_{\mathrm{KL}}^{\top}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{d}_{\mathcal{W}}=(\mathcal{W}_{11},\ldots,\mathcal{W}_{N_e,N_e})$ and $\mathbf{k}_{\mathrm{KL}}=(\alpha_{\mathrm{KL}}\mathrm{KL}_1,\ldots,\alpha_{\mathrm{KL}}\mathrm{KL}_{N_e})$ are row vectors, and $\mathrm{KL}_i$ is learner $i$'s teacher-to-student supervision term from \eqref{eq:fineft_pretrain}. Thus their matrix product is a scalar and retains the supervision coefficient. The cross-learner terms share experience among learners of similar dynamics while preventing experience poisoning across dissimilar ones.

\paragraph{Heuristic rationale for the selective update.} We do not claim a convergence theorem for the selective update; what follows is a mechanism-level rationale, stated together with the assumptions it relies on. In the pre-training phase, every learner is updated with the same local feasible-transition supervision; unlike a full account-state dynamic programme, Algorithm~\ref{alg:fineft_coav} does not represent accumulated wealth or liquidation state. For the selective-update phase, consider transitions from a single dynamic $z$: by the DP-MDP assumption of \sref{sec:fineft_dpmdp} they are approximately generated by a stationary MDP $M_z$, and the update of the best-fitting learner $i^\star$ on them is a Huber-TD update under $M_z$ with a non-negative supervision term. For the intended specialisation to follow, several further assumptions are needed: (i) a \emph{fixed assignment} of learners to dynamics, so that $i^\star$ does not switch while $z$ is active; (ii) a \emph{tabular} value representation; (iii) a discount $\gamma<1$; (iv) \emph{non-overlapping TD-error rankings} across dynamics, so that transitions from $z$ are consistently routed to the same learner; and (v) a \emph{monotone decrease} of each specialist's TD error on its own dynamic, so that the positive feedback loop described above is self-reinforcing. None of these is guaranteed by the algorithm itself: the assignment $i^\star=\argmin_i E_{ii}$ can switch as the networks evolve; a small TD residual need not indicate a small value error; the Huber loss shifts the fixed point away from the optimal action value precisely under the asymmetric, heavy-tailed noise that motivates its use; the cross-learner weights of Algorithm~\ref{alg:fineft_weight} are not normalised---rows of $\mathcal{W}$ can sum to more than one, so the coupling is not a convex combination---and they pull neighbouring learners towards each other's bootstrap targets; and the KL supervision draws all learners towards the same local teacher rather than towards per-dynamic optima. The deployed implementation, which uses function approximation, lies outside this tabular idealisation; the result should be read as motivation rather than a guarantee for the deployed algorithm.

\begin{algorithm}[!htb]
\caption{Construction of the Weight Matrix}
\label{alg:fineft_weight}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} ensemble TD-error matrix $\mathbf{E}\in\mathbb{R}^{N_e\times N_e}$, neighbourhood radius $\nu$
\State \textbf{Output:} weight matrix $\mathcal{W}$
\State Initialise $\mathcal{W}$ with shape $(N_e,N_e)$ and all elements $0$.
\State $i^\star \gets \argmin_i E_{ii}$; \; $i_{\min}\gets\max(i^\star-\nu,1)$; \; $i_{\max}\gets\min(i^\star+\nu,N_e)$
\If{$i_{\max} = i_{\min}$} \Comment{degenerate window ($\nu=0$ or $N_e=1$)}
  \State $\mathcal{W}_{i^\star i^\star} \gets 1$; \; \textbf{return} $\mathcal{W}$
\EndIf
\For{$i = i_{\min}$ \textbf{to} $i_{\max}$}
  \State $\mathcal{W}_{ii} \gets 1 - \dfrac{|i-i^\star|}{i_{\max}-i_{\min}}$ \Comment{diagonal decay}
\EndFor
\For{$i,j \gets 1$ \textbf{to} $N_e$}
  \If{$i \neq j$}
    \State $\mathcal{W}_{ij} \gets \min(\mathcal{W}_{ii},\mathcal{W}_{jj})\left(1 - \dfrac{|i-j|}{i_{\max}-i_{\min}}\right)$ \Comment{off-diagonal decay}
  \EndIf
\EndFor
\State \textbf{return} $\mathcal{W}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Stage II: Ensemble Filtering and Familiarity Models}
\label{sec:fineft_stage2}

After selecting a learner for each inferred dynamic, FineFT requires a signal indicating whether the current state resembles the states associated with that learner.

\paragraph{Filtering by profitability.} We divide the market into dynamics by slope, following \cref{ch:earnhft}: the series is low-pass filtered, segmented at local extrema, and adjacent chunks with similar slope are merged until stable, yielding $n_z$ dynamics. We back-test each learner across initial positions and retain, for each dynamic, the learner with the highest average profitability, producing a small, diverse, profitable pool.

\paragraph{Distributional familiarity proxy.} The paper uses the term \emph{capability boundary} for the support of a dynamic's estimated market-state distribution. Operationally, the released pipeline constructs this proxy from labelled validation segments and holds it fixed during test; the stage-III threshold is also tuned on validation. Reconstruction quality is a property of the state distribution rather than the policy: a well-reconstructed state need not be one on which the specialist performs well. Because the evaluation does not measure the association between VAE scores and held-out policy loss, the quantity is treated here as a distributional familiarity proxy rather than a calibrated competence set.

\paragraph{Familiarity scoring with VAEs.} We train a variational autoencoder~\cite{kingma2013auto} on the market-state representations of each dynamic. Its reconstruction loss is used as a heuristic out-of-distribution score. For $n_z$ dynamics we train $n_z$ VAEs, each on the state set $Y^i$ of its dynamic, minimising
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fineft_vae}
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{VAE}}^i(y)
&= \frac{1}{2}\sum_{d=1}^{D}
\left[
\frac{(y_d-\mu^{\mathrm{dec}}_d)^2}{(\sigma^{\mathrm{dec}}_d)^2}
+\log\!\bigl((\sigma^{\mathrm{dec}}_d)^2\bigr)+\log(2\pi)
\right] \\
&\quad+\mathrm{KL}\!\left(
\mathcal{N}\!\left(\mu^{\mathrm{enc}},\operatorname{diag}\!\bigl((\sigma^{\mathrm{enc}})^2\bigr)\right)
\,\middle\|\,
\mathcal{N}(0,I)
\right),\qquad y\in Y^i.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
the sum of the $D$ decoder negative log-likelihood terms and the standard-normal latent regulariser. Here $\mu_d^{\mathrm{dec}}$ and $(\sigma_d^{\mathrm{dec}})^2$ are the decoder's coordinate-wise output mean and variance, while $\mu^{\mathrm{enc}}$ and $(\sigma^{\mathrm{enc}})^2$ are the encoder parameters. The latent variable is sampled with the standard Gaussian reparameterisation $z=\mu^{\mathrm{enc}}+\sigma^{\mathrm{enc}}\odot\epsilon$, where $\epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)$. After fitting, the negative VAE loss provides the reference-score set $\mathcal{R}^i$ for each market dynamic.

\subsection{Stage III: Risk-Aware Heuristic Routing}
\label{sec:fineft_stage3}

\paragraph{Conservative policy.} When the router's maximum familiarity score is below its threshold, the released policy retains the current action while the peak-to-trough drawdown of the current non-flat holding episode is below $5\%$ and invokes the rule-based closing action once the drawdown reaches $5\%$. It does not open a new position in this fallback branch. In particular, an unfamiliar state does not cause an immediate exit: an existing leveraged position can remain open until the holding-episode drawdown threshold is reached.

\paragraph{Routing with VAE losses.} Rather than fit another policy, FineFT routes by a fixed rule. Given the $u$ most recent states $Y^u_T=(y_{T-u+1},\dots,y_T)$, each state's score under dynamic $i$ is its empirical-CDF position among the reference scores $\mathcal{R}^i$. The implementation smooths these scores with a \emph{normalised, finite-window} exponentially weighted average:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fineft_ema}
\eta^i_t = F_{\mathcal{R}^i}\!\left(-\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{VAE}}^i(y_t)\right),
\quad
\bar{\eta}^{i,u}_T =
\frac{\sum_{k=0}^{u-1}\rho^k \eta^i_{T-k}}
     {\sum_{k=0}^{u-1}\rho^k},
\end{equation}
where $F_{\mathcal{R}^i}$ is the empirical CDF of the reference-score set, $\rho\in(0,1)$, and the newest observation has weight one. The first $u$ environment steps fill the score deques while the rule-based closing policy acts. Thereafter, if $\max_i \bar{\eta}^{i,u}_T<\tau_{\mathrm{risk}}$, the fallback logic above is used; otherwise the specialist $i^\star=\argmax_i \bar{\eta}^{i,u}_T$ acts. Equality therefore selects a specialist. The three routing parameters $(u,\rho,\tau_{\mathrm{risk}})$ are tuned jointly on validation return. This is the second instantiation of the thesis's aggregation mechanism---a heuristic driven by generative distribution scores rather than a learned router.

\begin{algorithm}[!htb]
\caption{Risk-Aware Heuristic Routing}
\label{alg:fineft_routing}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} specialist pool $\Pi$, VAE models $\{\mathcal{V}^i\}_{i=1}^{n_z}$, reference sets $\{\mathcal{R}^i\}_{i=1}^{n_z}$
\Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent} state window $Y^u_T$, decay $\rho$, threshold $\tau_{\mathrm{risk}}$
\State \textbf{Output:} current strategy $\pi$
\For{dynamic $i = 1$ \textbf{to} $n_z$}
  \State Obtain state scores $\eta^i_t$ from $\mathcal{V}^i$, $Y^u_T$, and $\mathcal{R}^i$.
  \State Compute the normalised finite-window score $\bar{\eta}^{i,u}_T$ by \eqref{eq:fineft_ema}.
\EndFor
\State $i^\star \gets \argmax_i \bar{\eta}^{i,u}_T$
\If{$\bar{\eta}^{i^\star,u}_T < \tau_{\mathrm{risk}}$}
  \State \textbf{return} conservative policy $\pi^c$ \Comment{out-of-distribution}
\Else
  \State \textbf{return} specialist $\pi^{i^\star}$
\EndIf
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:fineft_setup}

\paragraph{Datasets.} We evaluate on four mainstream cryptocurrencies over two years, covering bull, bear, and sideways conditions (\tref{tab:fineft_dataset}). The underlying trades, quotes, derivatives tickers, and order-book snapshots were obtained from Tardis, which offers trial access and commercially available extended historical coverage.\footnote{\url{https://tardis.dev/}} The raw streams are downsampled to a uniform five-minute frequency. The published protocol uses the first year for training, the next six months for validation, and the final six months for testing.

Following the published protocol, market features aligned by timestamp are augmented with rolling indicators. Two filters then reduce the feature set: XGBoost importance removes low-importance variables, and Pearson correlation removes redundant ones. The resulting market state has about 300 dimensions. The validation period is used for ensemble filtering, VAE fitting, score calibration, and routing-hyperparameter selection.

\paragraph{Baselines.} We compare against twelve methods: two policy-based (PPO~\cite{schulman2017proximal}, and DRA, an LSTM-based PPO variant~\cite{briola2021deep}); two value-based (DQN~\cite{mnih2015human}, and CDQNRP~\cite{zhu2022quantitative}, the same method that appears under this name in \cref{ch:earnhft}); two rule-based (MACD~\cite{appel2005technical}, IV~\cite{chordia2002order}); two ensemble (EDQN, an ensemble DQN in the style of Averaged-DQN~\cite{anschel2017averaged}, and SUNRISE~\cite{lee2021sunrise}); one distributional (RAQR, a risk-averse quantile-regression DQN~\cite{dabney2018distributional}); and three hierarchical or routing-based methods. The last group contains a performance-based trading adaptation named after the Winnow linear-threshold algorithm~\cite{littlestone1988winnow}, EHFT (EarnHFT, \cref{ch:earnhft})~\cite{qin2024earnhft}, and MHFT (MacroHFT)~\cite{zong2024macrohft}. The same hyperparameters used by FineFT are applied to the baselines where applicable.

\paragraph{Training.} Experiments run on four RTX~4090 GPUs, completing in about six hours. The transaction cost is $0.02\%$, following Binance, with a leverage factor of $5$. FineFT's risk and risk-adjusted metrics are computed from daily returns obtained by grouping the 288 five-minute observations in a day, with annualisation constant $m=365$. We report the six metrics of \sref{sec:bg_metrics}. Routing hyperparameters are tuned separately for each market on validation data. The published evaluation additionally applies pairwise Wilcoxon signed-rank tests to daily aggregates across the test periods.

\subsection{Effectiveness of Risk-Aware Routing and Case Studies}

\paragraph{Computational cost.} Training the full FineFT pipeline---selective-update ensemble pre-training, per-dynamic VAE fitting, and validation-window hyperparameter tuning---takes approximately six hours on a server with four NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPUs and an AMD Ryzen Threadripper PRO 5995WX CPU. At inference time the cost is dominated by the joint evaluation of one DQN specialist together with the five VAEs that gate it. With PyTorch's \texttt{vmap}, joint inference completes in approximately $0.78\,\mathrm{ms}$ on a single RTX 4090, only marginally above the $0.28\,\mathrm{ms}$ of the bare DQN; both are several orders of magnitude below the five-minute decision interval, so the VAE machinery does not constrain the deployment frequency in practice.

\section{Chapter Summary}
\label{sec:fineft_summary}

This chapter presented FineFT, a three-stage risk-aware ensemble RL framework for leveraged futures. A selective-update mechanism trains a pool of specialists, a per-dynamic VAE models distributional familiarity, and a heuristic router chooses between a specialist and a drawdown-conditioned fallback. Averaged across the four crypto markets, FineFT records the highest total return and the lowest average MDD, reducing MDD by $42.5\%$ relative to RAQR in the complete baseline set. The selective-update study shows improved training efficiency and converged performance, while the routing ablation shows the benefit of combining specialists and the additional protection provided by the risk threshold. Relative to EarnHFT, FineFT replaces the learned router with a heuristic driven by generative distribution scores. The next chapter moves from trading to signal generation and removes the aggregation mechanism from the learning reward altogether.
