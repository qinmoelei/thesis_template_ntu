\chaptermark{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{ch:conclusion}

This thesis asked what reinforcement learning can do for quantitative trading, where it fails when moved from one trading task to another, and how those failures can be repaired. It answered the question through three studies that span the trading-frequency spectrum and progress from trade execution to signal generation. This final chapter summarises the contributions, revisits the perspective that unites them, states their limitations honestly, and sketches the work they invite.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Summary of Contributions}
\label{sec:concl_summary}

\paragraph{EarnHFT (\cref{ch:earnhft}).} For second-level high-frequency trading, the decisive obstacles are the extremely long decision horizon and regime non-stationarity. EarnHFT addressed the first with an optimal action-value teacher computed by dynamic programming over future prices. The construction rests on a small-agent assumption---that the trader's orders are too small to move prices---which is a standard modelling premise for historical-replay evaluation rather than an established property of the market. Under that exogenous-price assumption and a fixed historical training window, the teacher preserves the optimal Bellman fixed point of the supervised update in the tabular idealisation analysed in \cref{ch:earnhft}---the function-approximation implementation lies outside that guarantee---while accelerating learning empirically. EarnHFT addressed the second obstacle by training a pool of trend specialists and a minute-level router that switches among them. EarnHFT attains the highest total return on all four markets: on the two markets where the strongest baseline is profitable it exceeds the best baseline's total return by 39\% (GALA) and 110\% (BTC/TUSD), and it is the only profitable method on the remaining two; it also leads on the risk-adjusted metrics in three of the four markets. Ablations confirmed that the value supervisor is the more potent half of the teacher.

\paragraph{FineFT (\cref{ch:fineft}).} For leveraged futures, leverage amplifies the aleatoric noise of the reward and makes the absence of competence-awareness fatal. FineFT trained diverse specialists efficiently through a selective-update mechanism driven by an ensemble temporal-difference matrix. As a by-product, this rule segmented the market automatically. A per-dynamic variational autoencoder then captured each specialist's capability boundary, and the router fell back to a conservative policy when the market state was out of distribution. Within the literature surveyed in this thesis, FineFT is, to our knowledge, the first to use VAEs for out-of-distribution detection in RL for quantitative trading. Averaged across the four markets, FineFT reduces maximum drawdown by roughly 63\% relative to the runner-up among baselines with non-negligible market exposure while attaining the highest average total return among the learned methods, although it is not uniformly dominant on every individual market.

\paragraph{AlphaQD (\cref{ch:alphaqd}).} For formulaic alpha mining, the binding obstacle is reward design: pool-based RL miners couple the reward to an evolving pool, training the policy against a moving target and offering no finite-sample generalisation bound. AlphaQD diagnosed this coupling and removed it, training a graph-based GFlowNet against an archive-independent reward and relocating diversity into a MAP-Elites archive keyed on the normalised daily IC series, a novel return-source behaviour descriptor. The resulting selection rule admits a finite-sample ensemble bound whose statistical complexity term scales with the number of occupied archive cells rather than with the size of the formula space; the computational cost of mining, by contrast, still grows with the total number of candidate formulas evaluated. On A-share and U.S.\ benchmarks AlphaQD lay in the leading signal cluster on both markets, with low seed variance and turnover among the learned pool-style methods.

\section{The Unifying Perspective Revisited}
\label{sec:concl_perspective}

Across three tasks that differ in frequency, instrument, action space, and even in whether the agent acts or generates, a similar response to non-stationarity recurred: \emph{decompose} the problem into more homogeneous sub-problems, \emph{specialise} a component to each, and \emph{aggregate} the specialists at deployment. The analogy must be drawn with care, because the three instantiations are not the same construction. EarnHFT and FineFT decompose the market itself---by trend and by latent dynamic respectively---and their specialists are policies selected by a router; for them, decomposition is a direct way to recover, locally and approximately, the stationary-MDP assumption on which reinforcement learning depends. AlphaQD decomposes not the market but the space of formula behaviours: its ``specialists'' are formulas retained as cell elites of an archive, the archive is a search-and-selection structure rather than a learner, and the component that actually combines the formulas' outputs is a downstream ridge regression. The skeleton recurs, but in AlphaQD it serves diversity of the exported pool rather than local stationarity.

The more pointed lesson concerns the aggregation mechanism. The component that composes the specialists---a router that selects one in EarnHFT and FineFT, an archive that selects several for a downstream ridge combiner in AlphaQD---is also the component most easily mis-designed, because the natural impulse is to learn it jointly with the specialists or to fold its criterion into the learning reward---and either coupling can re-introduce, through the back door, the non-stationarity the decomposition was meant to dissolve. The three studies trace a deliberate retreat from this coupling. EarnHFT learns its router by reinforcement learning against the trading reward---the most coupled of the three designs. Its specialists are trained and frozen before the router is trained, so the coupling lies not in joint optimisation but in the router's own learning target: aggregation is itself a reward-maximising policy, fitted offline to a finite span of history and exposed to the same non-stationarity as the specialists it arbitrates. FineFT replaces the learned router with a heuristic one driven by generative out-of-distribution estimates, so aggregation no longer requires policy learning. AlphaQD removes the aggregation mechanism from the learning reward altogether: the generator's reward never reads the archive, and the archive never edits the reward. The payoff of this final decoupling is concrete---reward stationarity and a finite-sample generalisation bound. We advance this as a unifying design perspective---a working hypothesis distilled from three studies---rather than a proven general principle: \emph{in a decompose--specialise--aggregate design whose aggregation criterion depends on non-stationary market feedback, keeping the aggregation mechanism out of the learning target avoids destabilising that target}. The three studies were not designed as a controlled comparison of degrees of coupling, so the thesis does not establish that more decoupling causes better performance; and joint aggregation is not universally harmful---mixture-of-experts systems, for instance, train gating and experts jointly with success in other domains. The hypothesis is offered as a caution for settings like the ones studied here, not as a law.

A second thread is methodological reuse across the chapters, which lends the thesis coherence beyond the shared skeleton. The dynamic-programming optimal action value introduced in EarnHFT reappears, with a liquidation mask, as the demonstrator and supervisor that pre-trains FineFT's ensemble. The slope-based market segmentation of EarnHFT is reused to define FineFT's dynamics. And the very limitation of EarnHFT's and FineFT's pool-and-route designs---that routing or selection, when coupled to learning, destabilises it---is what motivates AlphaQD's complete decoupling. The three studies are thus not three disconnected applications but successive iterations on one idea.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:concl_limitations}

Each study rests on assumptions that bound its claims.

\paragraph{EarnHFT.} The optimal action-value teacher is exact only because, at high frequency, prices are assumed independent of the agent's orders; the construction would not hold for an agent large enough to move the market. EarnHFT is also a deliberately aggressive trader: its teacher conveys profit-oriented experience and little about risk, so it trails on some risk metrics. Both the teacher and the segmentation depend on hindsight price information available only on historical data.

\paragraph{FineFT.} The dynamic-parametric MDP assumption---that dynamics evolve slowly enough that a short window is approximately stationary---can be violated by a hard regime break. Because the market segmentation and the per-dynamic VAEs are fitted on the training and validation windows and held fixed through the test window, such a break would leave the capability-boundary estimates stale for the remainder of deployment; the framework performs no online recalibration. The conservative fallback is a heuristic stop rather than a learned policy, and the risk threshold must be tuned on validation data. Moreover, the calibration of the VAE out-of-distribution scores has not been directly validated: the routing threshold is selected on validation data, and the reliability of the scores under regimes absent from training is untested.

\paragraph{AlphaQD.} Invalid or numerically unstable formulas are absorbed by the reward floor rather than by a learned invalid-expression penalty, which suffices for the markets studied but is not a structural fix. The PCA descriptor basis and bin edges are fitted during a warm-up phase and thereafter refitted only when a preset episode-count trigger fires---a trigger that never fires in the reported runs---so a structural shift in the distribution of candidate behaviours after warm-up would leave the cell partition stale until the next refit. The generalisation results hold under idealised premises---most importantly a descriptor map held fixed after warm-up and a stylised selection rule---and are not claims of global optimality; they should be read as motivation for the design rather than as guarantees for the deployed system.

\paragraph{Common scope.} All three studies are evaluated offline on historical data, and none within a high-fidelity execution simulator. EarnHFT and FineFT act in market-replay environments that execute against recorded market data with proportional commission but without modelling latency, partial fills, or the agent's own market impact; AlphaQD is evaluated by a daily-frequency signal back-test with a twenty-trading-day horizon and rebalance, which involves no execution simulation at all. Live deployment would introduce latency, execution uncertainty, and impact effects that none of these evaluations capture.

\paragraph{Evaluation protocol.} Several further limitations concern the strength of the empirical evidence rather than the methods themselves. The test windows are short relative to the variety of market conditions---nine days per market for EarnHFT and six months per market for FineFT---and the EarnHFT results are single training runs reported without seed variance, confidence intervals, or significance tests. The validation windows are reused across many decisions---specialist selection, routing hyperparameters, and design choices alike---which risks selection overfitting, and the FineFT hyperparameter sensitivity analysis is measured on a test window, which constitutes a form of test-set reuse. In AlphaQD, the equity universes are not defined point-in-time, so survivorship bias cannot be excluded, particularly for the Russell-3000-style U.S.\ panel, and the twenty-day forward labels are not purged or embargoed around the split boundaries, leaving residual overlap between adjacent windows. Finally, the theoretical results of \cref{ch:earnhft} and \cref{ch:alphaqd} are proved for idealised versions of the algorithms; the deployed implementations lie partly outside these idealisations, so the theory should be read as motivating the designs rather than certifying them.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:concl_future}

\paragraph{Reflecting decoupling back onto execution.} The clearest direction is to carry AlphaQD's lesson back to execution: EarnHFT's learned router and FineFT's heuristic router could be replaced by a structural, archive-style aggregation mechanism whose construction never enters the specialists' reward, testing whether alpha mining's reward-stationarity and generalisation benefits transfer to execution.

\paragraph{A unified cross-frequency framework.} The three studies share a skeleton but not an implementation. A single framework that instantiates decompose--specialise--aggregate with task-appropriate decomposition, specialists, and decoupled aggregation across the whole frequency spectrum would turn the thesis's perspective into a reusable system.

\paragraph{Richer descriptors and generators.} AlphaQD's behaviour descriptor could be augmented with additional economically meaningful axes, such as factor-style exposure, and its GFlowNet generator could be replaced by a language-model symbolic policy while preserving the archive-independent reward, so the archive organises diversity rather than becoming a moving component of the reward.

\paragraph{Risk and uncertainty as first-class objectives.} FineFT separated aleatoric from epistemic uncertainty and handled each with a dedicated mechanism. Treating both as first-class objectives throughout---distributional value learning for the former and generative competence estimation for the latter, jointly optimised rather than bolted on---would unify the risk machinery developed in this thesis.

\paragraph{Toward deployment.} Finally, closing the gap to live trading---modelling latency and market impact, and validating the decoupled-aggregation designs under realistic execution---is the necessary step from these methods to systems that operate in real markets.

\bigskip
\noindent Reinforcement learning is a natural language for quantitative trading, but only once the non-stationarity, risk, and reward-design pathologies of financial markets are confronted directly. This thesis confronted them for three concrete tasks and distilled from them a working hypothesis: decompose the problem, specialise to its parts, and aggregate the specialists with a mechanism kept deliberately apart from what they learn---a design perspective to be tested further, not a proven general principle.
