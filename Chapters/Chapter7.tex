\chaptermark{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{ch:conclusion}

This thesis asked what reinforcement learning can do for quantitative trading, where it fails when moved from one trading task to another, and how those failures can be mitigated. It answered the question through three studies spanning second-level position control, five-minute leveraged trading, and daily signal generation. This final chapter summarises the contributions, revisits the comparative perspective that connects them, states their scope and limitations, and sketches the work they invite.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Summary of Contributions}
\label{sec:concl_summary}

\paragraph{EarnHFT (\cref{ch:earnhft}).} For second-level target-position control in historical order-book replay, referred to in this thesis as high-frequency trading, EarnHFT combines a sample-path optimal Q-teacher with trend-conditioned policies and an offline minute-level router. Across four dense second-level replays, EarnHFT attains the highest total return on all four datasets, and the component ablation supports the usefulness of the optimal Q-teacher and optimal actor. The replay does not model latency, queue priority, or endogenous market impact and therefore does not evaluate a live order-execution system.

\paragraph{FineFT (\cref{ch:fineft}).} For leveraged futures, FineFT uses an ensemble TD matrix to assign updates selectively, fits a VAE to each inferred dynamic, and routes by heuristic reconstruction-based distributional-familiarity scores with a conservative fallback. These scores are not calibrated probabilities of policy failure. Across the four crypto markets, FineFT has the highest average total return among learned methods and the lowest average maximum drawdown. The selective-update and routing ablations further support the two principal components: selective training improves the efficiency--performance trade-off, while the VAE router and risk threshold combine complementary specialists and reduce downside exposure.

\paragraph{AlphaQD (\cref{ch:alphaqd}).} For formulaic alpha mining, AlphaQD separates a candidate-only GFlowNet reward from a MAP-Elites archive keyed on the normalised daily IC-series shape. This removes archive membership from the reward and provides a structured export set. The chapter develops analytical motivations under conditional or idealised premises: an abstract equal-weight ICIR bound and a separate finite-class comparison conditional on a realised archive. Neither is a finite-sample guarantee for the fitted ridge portfolio or the complete data-dependent pipeline. The method is evaluated on A-share and U.S.\ panels, with its strongest portfolio result on A-shares.

\section{The Unifying Perspective Revisited}
\label{sec:concl_perspective}

Across tasks that differ in frequency, instrument, and output, the methods all structure heterogeneity, construct differentiated candidates, and aggregate them. The analogy must be drawn with care. EarnHFT groups historical periods by trend; FineFT assigns transitions to ensemble members from their TD errors; AlphaQD partitions the behaviour space of formulas, not the market. Its cell elites are formulas rather than RL specialists, and a downstream ridge regression combines their outputs. The comparison is therefore an organising vocabulary, not evidence that all three recover locally stationary MDPs.

The narrower connective thread concerns target coupling. EarnHFT trains its router only after freezing the low-level policies; FineFT uses a rule over reconstruction scores; AlphaQD keeps archive membership out of the generator's reward. This motivates a conditional working hypothesis: when aggregation would change a candidate's target as other candidates enter or leave the set, separating the aggregation criterion can stabilise that target and clarify the assumptions behind selection. The studies were neither designed nor executed as a controlled comparison of coupling, so they do not establish a causal performance benefit, and jointly trained mixture-of-experts systems remain valid counterexamples to any universal rule.

There is also concrete methodological reuse. FineFT reuses EarnHFT's hindsight supervision based on dynamic programming and its slope-based segmentation, while adapting both to leveraged futures and ensemble training. AlphaQD then relocates diversity and set selection outside the generator's reward. These are related iterations, but conceptual lineage should not be confused with identical implementations.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:concl_limitations}

Each study rests on assumptions that bound its claims.

\paragraph{EarnHFT.} The optimal Q-teacher and trend labels use future prices and are available only during historical training. Replay makes prices exactly exogenous to the simulated action, but that is only a small-agent approximation to live trading and cannot support a claim about market impact or optimal order execution. The released learner takes a maximum directly over the target network, so despite the DDQN naming it is not the strict Double-DQN target.

\paragraph{FineFT.} The VAE reconstruction score is a heuristic measure of distributional familiarity, not a calibrated failure probability. The supplementary Corn/ES results also use a lower frequency than the main cryptocurrency experiments. The results establish the value of the framework under the reported protocols, while broader frequency and asset-class transfer remain open questions.

\paragraph{AlphaQD.} The descriptor is warmed up on 2,000 finite candidates and its PCA basis and bin edges are periodically updated, so the fixed-map analysis is an idealised result rather than a guarantee for every intermediate archive. The final selection-complexity result is conditional on a realised archive, and the equal-weight ICIR bound motivates the diversity mechanism without directly characterising the ridge-weighted portfolio. These distinctions delimit the theoretical claims while leaving the empirical comparison unchanged.

\paragraph{Common scope.} All three studies are evaluated on historical data. EarnHFT and FineFT focus on position-control policies, while AlphaQD evaluates daily predictive signals and a downstream portfolio. The resulting tables compare methods under their respective shared protocols; absolute metrics should not be compared across the three tasks.

\paragraph{Evaluation protocol.} The three studies use different markets, frequencies, and evaluation spans, so their absolute metrics are not compared across chapters. FineFT's AVOL values are annualised, its MDD comparison uses the complete baseline set including RAQR, and AlphaQD follows the AlphaGen train--validation--test protocol with a common validation-fitted ridge combiner. The theoretical results should be read under their explicit assumptions, alongside the corresponding empirical evidence.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:concl_future}

\paragraph{Testing reward separation in trading.} A direct follow-up is to compare learned, heuristic, and structural aggregation under a controlled position-trading experiment. Such a study could test whether keeping set membership out of a specialist's reward improves target stability; the present three studies do not establish that result.

\paragraph{A unified cross-frequency framework.} The three studies share a vocabulary but not one mathematical construction. A useful framework would make the distinctions explicit---market regime, transition subset, or formula behaviour---and test candidate construction and aggregation independently at each frequency.

\paragraph{Richer descriptors and generators.} AlphaQD's behaviour descriptor could be augmented with additional economically meaningful axes, such as factor-style exposure, and its GFlowNet generator could be replaced by a language-model symbolic policy while preserving the archive-independent reward, so the archive organises diversity rather than becoming a moving component of the reward.

\paragraph{Risk and uncertainty as first-class objectives.} Future work should distinguish transition noise, model uncertainty, distributional familiarity, and realised account risk. Calibrated familiarity measures could further improve the VAE-based routing mechanism and make its risk signal easier to interpret.

\paragraph{Unified evaluation.} A final direction is to study the three aggregation mechanisms under a shared experimental framework. Controlling candidate capacity, computation budget, and market window would allow learned routing, familiarity-based routing, and archive-based selection to be compared directly.

\bigskip
\noindent Reinforcement learning provides a useful framework for the sequential trading and signal-generation problems studied in this thesis. Across three distinct tasks, the thesis develops task-specific ways to structure heterogeneity, construct differentiated candidates, and aggregate them. This perspective summarises the three contributions and motivates future work on robust quantitative decision making.
