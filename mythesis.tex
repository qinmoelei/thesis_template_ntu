\abstract{\addtocontents{toc}{\vspace{0.8em}}}% Add a gap in the Contents, for aesthetics

% 1. The Problem 2. The Issues 3. The Approach 4. The Results 5. The Experiments 6. The Applications

Quantitative trading converts market data into trading decisions and is, at its core, a sequential decision-making problem under uncertainty. Reinforcement learning (RL) is therefore a natural modelling tool, yet financial markets violate the assumptions on which standard RL rests: they are non-stationary, their reward signals carry a low signal-to-noise ratio, the decision horizons can be extreme, and a single mistake can be financially catastrophic. This thesis studies what RL can and cannot do across the breadth of the quantitative-trading stack. It is organised around three tasks that span the trading-frequency spectrum and progress from trade execution to signal generation: second-level high-frequency trading of a single spot asset, minute-level trading of leveraged cryptocurrency futures, and daily-frequency formulaic alpha mining over a full cross-section of equities. For each task, the thesis isolates the specific obstacle that prevents off-the-shelf RL from succeeding and develops a method that removes it.

The first contribution, \textbf{EarnHFT}, addresses high-frequency trading, where the trajectory length (of the order of one million steps per month) cripples data efficiency and rapid regime changes break any single policy. EarnHFT is a three-stage hierarchical RL framework that computes an optimal action-value teacher by dynamic programming to accelerate and stabilise second-level training, assembles a pool of trend-specialised agents, and trains a minute-level router that switches among them. It exceeds the strongest baseline by at least 30\% in profitability across four cryptocurrency markets. The second contribution, \textbf{FineFT}, moves to leveraged futures, where leverage amplifies the aleatoric noise of the reward and where an agent confident outside its competence can be liquidated. FineFT is a three-stage ensemble framework that trains specialists by a selective-update rule driven by an ensemble temporal-difference matrix, learns the capability boundary of each specialist with per-dynamic variational autoencoders, and routes between specialists and a conservative fallback by out-of-distribution detection. It reduces risk by more than 40\% while remaining the most profitable learned method. The third contribution, \textbf{AlphaQD}, turns from execution to signal generation: it diagnoses the boosting-style reward of pool-based RL alpha miners as a source of reward non-stationarity and of an absent generalisation guarantee, and replaces it with a quality-diversity formulation in which a GFlowNet is trained against an archive-independent reward while a MAP-Elites archive, keyed on the normalised daily information-coefficient series as a return-source descriptor, organises diversity and selection. On full-market Chinese A-share and U.S.\ benchmarks it attains the leading signal-quality cluster on both markets with the lowest seed-to-seed variance and turnover among learned pool-style methods.

Read together, the three works trace a single methodological pattern --- decompose a non-stationary market into stationary sub-problems, learn a set of specialists, and compose them through a selector --- and a clear lesson about where the selector belongs: coupling the act of selection into the learning target re-introduces the non-stationarity it was meant to cure, so the selector is progressively decoupled from the policy reward across the three chapters, from a learned online router, to a generative risk-aware router, to a fully structural archive. The thesis thus offers both a set of state-of-the-art methods for distinct trading tasks and a unifying perspective on how reinforcement learning should be structured for non-stationary financial markets.
