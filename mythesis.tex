\abstract{\addtocontents{toc}{\vspace{0.8em}}}% Add a gap in the Contents, for aesthetics

% 1. The Problem 2. The Issues 3. The Approach 4. The Results 5. The Experiments 6. The Applications

Financial markets attract vast pools of capital worldwide. Quantitative trading converts market data into trading decisions---at heart, sequential decision making under uncertainty, for which reinforcement learning (RL), optimising a long-horizon return rather than a one-step prediction, is a natural tool. Markets, however, violate the assumptions on which standard RL rests: distributions drift, rewards have low signal-to-noise ratios, decision horizons can be extremely long, and errors can have asymmetric financial consequences. This thesis studies that promise--deployment gap through three tasks spanning both frequency and function: second-level position control for a single spot asset, five-minute trading of leveraged cryptocurrency futures, and daily formulaic alpha mining over a full cross-section of equities. For each task we identify the main obstacle to off-the-shelf RL and propose a method that addresses it.

First, we study second-level high-frequency trading, where an agent faces horizons on the order of one million steps and rapid regime changes challenge a monolithic policy. We propose EarnHFT, a three-stage hierarchical RL framework: (i)~a hindsight, sample-path Q-teacher computed by dynamic programming over replayed future prices to provide auxiliary supervision for second-level training; (ii)~a pool of trend-specialised agents trained under different preference parameters and selected by profitability; and (iii)~a minute-level router, trained offline and frozen at deployment, that picks a specialist as conditions change. In the reported single-seed historical replays of four cryptocurrency markets, EarnHFT attains the highest total return in every dataset.

Second, we investigate leveraged futures trading on five-minute bars, where leverage magnifies the profit-and-loss consequences of noisy returns and unfamiliar states can precipitate liquidation. We propose FineFT, a three-stage ensemble RL framework: (i)~a selective-update mechanism, driven by an ensemble temporal-difference matrix, that trains diverse specialists efficiently and is intended to encourage differentiated specialists; (ii)~one variational autoencoder per inferred market dynamic to provide a reconstruction-based familiarity score; and (iii)~a risk-aware heuristic router that switches between specialists and a conservative fallback using recent VAE losses. Across the four reported markets, FineFT has the highest average total return among the learned methods and the lowest unfiltered average maximum drawdown, although it is not uniformly dominant on every individual market.

Third, turning from trading to signal generation, we study formulaic alpha mining, where a dominant family of pool-based RL miners couples each candidate's reward to an evolving pool, creating a moving target. We propose AlphaQD, a three-stage quality-diversity RL method: (i)~a relational-graph GFlowNet generator trained by trajectory balance against an archive-independent terminal reward; (ii)~a Return-Source MAP-Elites archive whose behaviour descriptor is the normalised daily information-coefficient series of each formula; and (iii)~a top-$k$ cell-selection rule that exports one high-quality representative per selected cell as the deployed pool. The method is evaluated on full-market Chinese A-share and U.S.\ panels; on A-shares it attains the highest annualised return among the learned formula-mining methods, while the U.S.\ results are reported as a cross-market robustness check rather than evidence of causal alpha attribution.

The three studies share a broader design move: structure a heterogeneous task into distinguishable regions or behaviours, construct candidates specialised to those regions, and aggregate them through a task-appropriate mechanism. The objects differ materially---market-trend policies in EarnHFT, selectively updated learners in FineFT, and formula elites organised by return-source behaviour in AlphaQD---so the pattern is a comparative perspective, not a claim that all three decompose the market into stationary MDPs. Across the studies, aggregation is progressively separated from the generator's learning reward: an offline-trained RL router in EarnHFT, a reconstruction-based heuristic router in FineFT, and a quality-diversity archive followed by a ridge combiner in AlphaQD.
